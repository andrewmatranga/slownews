Investigator Profile

Who I Am

I am a professor of journalism and media studies at the University of Denver, where I teach courses in journalism, editorial design, data visualization, brand journalism, media production, and civic media literacy. My work sits at the intersection of journalism practice, design thinking, curriculum, public life, and the habits of attention students need to navigate contemporary information environments.

I am also a Doctor of Education, with research focused on teaching presence, flipped learning, reflective practice, and the ways instructors design learning environments that help students move from information exposure to meaningful understanding. Slow News, Smart Citizens grows out of that work: it is a teaching framework, a media literacy practice, and a civic reading habit.

The project begins with a simple belief: students do not only need more information. They need better habits for reading, questioning, comparing, and responding to information.

The Reading Life Behind the Curriculum

Why Newspapers Matter Here

This project is also personal. I grew up reading newspapers and have spent much of my life studying how publications organize public attention. A printed newspaper or magazine is not nostalgic decoration in this curriculum. It is a teaching object. It lets students see hierarchy, adjacency, emphasis, omission, design, and editorial judgment in ways a personalized feed often hides.

The curriculum does not require every student to subscribe to a daily paper. It asks students and instructors to find a shared reading object: a print newspaper, a magazine, a trade publication, a hard copy of an academic journal, or a digital replica edition that preserves the logic of a printed issue. The point is not paper for paper’s sake. The point is visible editorial intention.

The practical commitment

The project is designed to be usable by instructors who do not have journalism training, students who do not have paid subscriptions, and courses that only have a small amount of time to devote to media literacy.

Principal Investigator Statement

What I Am Trying to Build

Slow News, Smart Citizens is a curriculum for reading public life with care. It is built for a moment when students encounter news, summaries, search results, platform recommendations, synthetic media, and public claims in overlapping streams. The answer is not to retreat from technology or to romanticize print. The answer is to practice better habits of attention.

The project uses newspapers, magazines, journals, and replica editions because they make editorial choices visible. It uses notebooks because handwriting slows down thinking. It uses discussions because interpretation improves when students test ideas together. It uses assignments and rubrics because media literacy has to be teachable, assessable, and adaptable beyond one instructor.

The goal is not simply to teach students how to spot bad information. The goal is to help them become the kind of readers and citizens who can tell what is known, what is missing, what is at stake, and what a responsible response might require.

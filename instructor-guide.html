Slow News, Smart Citizens — A Framework for Any Classroom
Reading the News Together, on Purpose
A media literacy practice that fits in any course, in any discipline, taught at any scale.
Pick it up for a single class session. Run it as a recurring weekly ritual. Build a full course around it. The framework scales to whatever you have room for — and the conversation it starts tends to go further than you expect.
The Idea
The Coffee Klatch
Before it was a curriculum, this was a practice. A group of people sitting around a table, each with something they read, talking about what it means. No agenda, no expert at the front of the room — just the shared act of paying attention to what is happening in the world and thinking out loud about it together.
That practice is older than journalism education. It is older than universities. And it turns out to be exactly what most students are not getting: a low-stakes, unhurried space to engage with the news as curious human beings rather than consumers or critics.
Imagine your class, on any given Tuesday, opening with five questions. Not on a slide. Just in the air.
01
What did you read this week that surprised you?
02
Where did you find it — and how did it find you?
03
What does your field know about this that the story doesn't say?
04
Who isn't in this story?
05
What would you need to know to trust it?
Those five questions are the whole curriculum, in compressed form. Everything else — the reading journals, the structured assignments, the collaborative capstone — is scaffolding around the practice of asking them regularly, with other people, about real things happening right now.
"You do not need to redesign your course. You need fifteen minutes at the start of class and students who brought something to read."
The modern twist is that two of those five questions — where did you find it, and how did it find you — now open onto something genuinely new and worth talking about. Most of what students encounter as news in 2026 was selected, summarized, or generated by systems they cannot see, optimizing for attention rather than understanding. That fact, named plainly and without drama, is the entry point to everything the framework asks.
The AI Question
What to Say When Students Ask About AI and the News
You do not need to be an AI expert to teach this framework. You need to be honest about what is happening — which is not complicated, even if it is unsettling.
A Plain Account of the Moment
For instructors — adapt freely for your students and your discipline

The information environment students navigate today is substantially shaped by AI systems — not in a futuristic sense, but right now. Recommendation algorithms decide what appears in their feeds. AI tools summarize articles before students read them. A growing portion of online content is generated, in whole or in part, by large language models. Some of that content is accurate and useful. Some of it is fabricated. Most of it exists somewhere between — plausible on the surface, missing the sourcing and context that would let a reader evaluate it properly.

At the same time, the companies building these tools are driving significant economic growth. This is worth naming because students are living inside both realities simultaneously: an information environment that is harder to read clearly, and an economy that depends on the tools making it harder. That is not a political observation. It is a structural one, and students generally recognize it as describing their own experience.

The framework does not ask students to be against AI or to distrust everything they read. It asks them to slow down enough to notice where information comes from, how it traveled, and what it is asking of them as readers and citizens. That is a very old ask. The AI moment makes it more urgent, not different in kind.

How directly you engage the AI question depends on your course and your students. The Small version holds it lightly — as context, introduced once, returned to when it surfaces naturally. The Medium version makes it a recurring thread. The Large version builds it into the course's central inquiry. All three versions start from the same honest premise: the newspaper on the table and the feed on the phone are not equivalent objects, and it is worth knowing why.
Three things worth saying to students, in any version

On speed: The news cycle moves faster than understanding. Slowing down is not falling behind — it is the condition for knowing what you think.

On AI-generated content: The threat to a well-informed public is not dramatic fabrication. It is ordinary noise — content that is not quite sourced, not quite accurate, not quite authored by anyone, present everywhere. Learning to notice it is a skill, and skills improve with practice.

On their own knowledge: Whatever they are studying, they know things about their field that most journalists covering it do not. That knowledge is not background — it is a contribution. The framework asks them to bring it into the room.

Small, Medium, Large
Choose What Fits Your Course Right Now
Each size is a complete version of the framework. Pick one based on what you actually have room for — not what you wish you had room for. You can always grow into a larger version later. The Small version done well is better than the Large version done under pressure.
Small
The Opening Conversation
One session, or a recurring ten-minute ritual at the start of class. No assignments. No redesign.
+
Time: 10–15 min per session, or 1–3 dedicated sessions
Student prep: Read something. Bring it.
Assessment: None required
The simplest version of this framework is also the most durable. At the start of class — any class, any week — you ask students what they read. Then you ask where they found it. Then you ask one of the five questions above. That is it. That is the practice.
Done consistently across a semester, even for ten minutes a week, it builds something: a class that shows up with things to say, that knows the difference between a story and a headline, and that has started noticing how their field lives in the public conversation.
One-Session Version — Stand-Alone
Reading the Room: News, AI, and What We Actually Know

A single dedicated class session that introduces the five questions, practices description on a shared example, and opens the AI conversation. Works in any discipline. Requires nothing from students in advance except showing up.

  • Open (5 min): Ask students where they got their news this week. List the sources on the board — or just in the air. Do not evaluate them yet.
  • The shared read (10 min): Bring one current news item related to your field. Read it together — aloud or briefly in silence. Then ask: what does this story actually say, according to the evidence it cites?
  • The source question (10 min): Where did this story come from? Who reported it? What is the primary source — the study, the document, the event — and how far did it travel before becoming this article? Did AI touch it anywhere along the way?
  • The field question (10 min): What does your discipline know about this topic that the story does not say? What would a specialist add — or correct?
  • Close (5 min): What question does the story leave open? Not a rhetorical question — a real one that a careful reader would still have after finishing it.
Recurring Ritual Version
The Opening Ten Minutes, Every Week

Reserve the first ten minutes of class each week for the coffee klatch. One student brings something they read — from anywhere, in any format — and the class spends ten minutes with it using some version of the five questions. Rotate the student who brings the item. No grades attached; the practice is the point.

  • Keep a running list of sources students have cited across the semester — it becomes a portrait of the class's information diet.
  • When AI-generated or AI-summarized content appears (it will), name it without drama and ask: how would you know? What would you look for?
  • Let the conversation go where it goes. The field question — what does your discipline know about this? — is the one that tends to surprise students most.
What instructors say after trying this

The most common response: students start bringing better things. By Week 4, they are not just sharing what appeared in their feed — they are tracing stories, comparing sources, noticing gaps. The ten minutes does its own teaching.

Medium
The Story Arc
Four to five weeks. Students follow one story across the news cycle and write about what they find.
+
Time: 4–5 weeks, in-class and outside
Student prep: Weekly reading + a brief journal entry
Assessment: Two short written pieces
The Medium version adds one thing to the coffee klatch: a thread. Students choose a story or topic they will follow across four or five weeks — not a research topic, but a live news story unfolding in real time. They watch it develop, notice how the coverage changes, and bring it to the weekly conversation from a position of sustained attention rather than fresh exposure.
The difference between someone who has been following a story for three weeks and someone who just read about it for the first time is visible in the room. The Medium version cultivates that difference deliberately, without overwhelming students or requiring them to become experts.
Week 1
Finding Your Story
Students choose a live news story in any field connected to their interests or major. They describe it — just describe it, without evaluation — and identify where the story is coming from. Where is the primary source? What happens to it between origin and headline?
Week 2
Watching It Move
A week later, the story has developed. Students track what changed: what new evidence appeared, whose voices entered or left the coverage, how the framing shifted. The AI question enters here naturally — was the summary they got this week different from what the reported story said?
Week 3
Bringing What You Know
Students apply their disciplinary knowledge to the story. What does their field understand about this that the coverage does not explain? This is the interpretive move — not opinion, but the contribution that comes from actually knowing something about the subject.
Week 4–5
Saying Something About It
Students write a short piece — 400–600 words — for a general reader: what this story is actually about, in light of what they now know about it. Not a summary, not a reaction. An account that only someone who has been paying attention for a month can give.
Weekly JournalA brief entry (150–200 words) before each class: what the student saw in their story this week, in the four-beat format. Completion-graded.
Source MapA short written piece (300–400 words) tracing the story's information architecture: where it comes from, how it travels, where AI enters.
The Short Piece400–600 words for a general reader. The student's interpretive contribution — what they know that the coverage does not say.
Weekly DiscussionIn-class: the coffee klatch conversation, now informed by four weeks of individual attention. Each student arrives knowing something the others do not.
Works in any discipline

A biology student follows coverage of a clinical trial. A history student follows a commemoration or anniversary story. An economics student follows a policy debate. A literature student follows a cultural controversy. The method is the same; the knowledge each brings to it is different — and that difference is the point.

Large
The Citizen Scholar
Ten weeks. A beat, five critical moves, a collective document the class makes together.
+
Time: Full 10-week quarter
Student prep: Weekly journal + readings + class ritual
Assessment: Four unit assignments + collective capstone
The Large version is the full Citizen Scholar framework: ten weeks, five critical moves (Description, Analysis, Interpretation, Evaluation, Engagement), four individual unit assignments, and a collective capstone — a class-produced document called the State of the World.
The coffee klatch is still at the center. Every class session opens with the Huddle — a student-run fifteen-minute ritual where two or three students brief the room on their beat thread, and anyone can bring something from outside the course to the Open Floor. By Week 3 the instructor is no longer starting class. By Week 6 the room is making connections the instructor did not plan. By Week 10 the class has produced something together that none of them could have made alone.
The full Canvas shell — all ten weeks, all pages, all rubrics and prompts — is available separately. What follows is the essential shape of the course.
Weeks 1–2
Description
Students choose a beat, map its source architecture, and identify a specific story thread they will follow for the rest of the quarter.
Weeks 3–4
Analysis
Students track their thread across the news cycle, map whose voices are present and absent, and identify the structural pattern in how their beat gets covered.
Weeks 5–6
Interpretation
Disciplinary knowledge applied to meaning. Students write the Interpretive Brief. The weekly Huddle begins surfacing connections between beats — clusters start to form.
Weeks 7–8
Evaluation
Ethical and equity analysis. Who bears the costs of what is happening in the thread? Does the coverage serve public understanding of those stakes? Clusters confirm and begin planning their State of the World entry.
Weeks 9–10
Engagement
Cluster drafts, peer review, revision, and assembly. Week 10 is the assembly session — the class puts together what it has been watching, and the document exists.
The State of the World

At Week 10, the class produces a shared document — the State of the World — assembled from cluster entries, each addressing the shared question that connected a group of beats. It can be a Google Doc, a printed zine, a Notion page, a simple PDF. What it is matters less than the fact that it exists: a record of what this particular class was watching, at this particular moment, and what they understood about it that most people did not.

What the Large version requires of instructors

One short lecture video per unit (five total, 10–12 minutes each). One current example selected per unit. Cluster groupings proposed after Week 6. The assembly session facilitated in Week 10. Everything else runs from the Canvas shell.

Getting Started
Three Things Before Tuesday
Whichever size you choose, the first session is the same. You need three things.
Before Your First Session
  • One news item from your field, from this week. It does not need to be the most important story. It needs to be one you find genuinely interesting and that connects, however obliquely, to what your course is about. Print it or have it ready to share on screen.
  • The five questions. You do not need them on a slide. You need them in your head: What does it say? Where did it come from? What does your field know about this? Who is missing? What would you need to trust it?
  • A light hand. The coffee klatch works when the instructor is a participant, not a facilitator. Bring your own answer to the five questions. Let the conversation find its own level. If it goes somewhere unexpected, follow it.
The rest follows from that. Students who spend fifteen minutes in a genuine conversation about a real news story, with someone who is genuinely curious about it alongside them, tend to show up the next week with something they found themselves. That is the practice taking root.
"The conversation is the curriculum. Everything else is structure to make sure it happens."

A final note on the AI conversation

At some point in the first few sessions, a student will mention that they found a story through an AI summary, or that they are not sure whether what they read was written by a person. When that happens — and it will — resist the urge to make it a lesson about AI. Let it be what it actually is: a moment of honest uncertainty about where information comes from and how to evaluate it. That uncertainty is not a problem to solve. It is the starting point of the whole conversation.

Stay curious about it alongside them. That is the whole method, in one sentence.

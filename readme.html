What This Project Is

Slow News, Smart Citizens is a modular media literacy curriculum built around a five-step critical process. It was developed at the University of Denver in spring 2026, with support from the Center for Ethical Artificial Intelligence, for use in media, film, and journalism courses at the undergraduate level. It is designed to be taught by one instructor, adapted by many others, and used across disciplines from journalism to the sciences to the humanities.

The curriculum has two layers that work together. The first is digital: a reading list, discussion boards, and written assignments that develop students' capacity to read the information environment critically. The second is analog: a hand-kept notebook, weekly observations made with pen and paper, and a final physical publication assembled by the class together. Neither layer is optional. Both are the point.

The project is organized around three sizes: Small, Medium, and Large. Those sizes describe the scope of the module an instructor chooses, not the physical format of anything. The Large version is designed for a journalism course at the quarter or semester level. The Medium and Small versions are designed to drop into any course, in any discipline, without restructuring the existing syllabus.

"Technology changes the conditions. Human beings determine what they do inside them. This curriculum is practice for making those determinations deliberately."

The Argument

The Thesis

What this curriculum is for, why the analog layer exists, and what the slow news practice is trying to build.

The Problem This Curriculum Addresses

The information environment has been changing in ways that make careful, engaged reading more difficult and more important at the same time. Social media optimized for engagement rather than accuracy or civic value. The feed replaced the editor. The algorithm replaced the front page. And now generative AI has introduced something different again: a synthesis layer that produces answers without sources, collapsing the chain from claim to origin that journalists and careful readers have always used to evaluate what they read.

At the same time, the default mode of information consumption has shifted from opt-in to opt-out. Earlier media technologies required some active choice on the reader's part: you bought the newspaper, you turned on the television, you searched for something. The emerging model of AI-generated summaries and agentic overviews arrives without being sought. You are opting out of it, increasingly, rather than opting in. That shift is worth naming as a structural fact, not a complaint.

Media literacy education has been addressing a version of this problem since the 1970s. The questions it has always asked are still the right questions: Who made this? For whom? What is left out? What does it assume? What would a different perspective reveal? The new conditions make those questions harder to apply — because the source is hidden, the author is unclear, and the volume of content is so large that careful reading of any individual piece feels like a luxury. Slow News, Smart Citizens is a response to those conditions. It does not try to reverse them. It tries to build the practice of reading carefully inside them.

The Two-Layer Structure

The curriculum has two layers because one layer is not enough. A purely digital media literacy curriculum teaches the critical process but operates entirely inside the medium that is accelerating past it. A purely analog curriculum teaches slowing down but disconnects students from the environment they actually need to navigate. Both layers are required, and they serve different functions.

The digital layer (the Canvas pages, the reading list, the discussion boards, the written assignments) develops the cognitive habits that media literacy requires: tracing sources, naming structures, applying disciplinary knowledge, making accountable judgments. This is the work students do at a screen, submitted digitally, assessed with rubrics.

The analog layer, which includes the hand-kept notebook, weekly observations written with pen on paper, and the physical publication assembled at the end, develops the embodied habits that the digital layer cannot. Writing by hand slows the recording process in a way that forces synthesis. You cannot write down everything, so you decide what matters. That decision is, in a small way, the same act as the critical process. The physical publication makes the work real in a way that a Canvas submission does not: it exists in the room, it can be held, it was made by hand by a specific group of people at a specific moment.

"The physical publication is not a nostalgia exercise. It is a direct, hand-made counterargument to the feed — which is what survived when the iPad failed to save the magazine."

Those two layers are not in tension with each other. They are the same project from different angles. The digital work trains students to notice things. The analog work gives them a place to record what they notice, slow down with it, and make something from it that will outlast the course.

The Coffee Klatch

There is a form of conversation that predates every media technology: people sitting down together, having read something, to talk about what they noticed. A coffee klatch. A reading group. A newsroom morning meeting. A kitchen table argument about something in the paper. The form is ancient, and in some ways it is the most durable thing journalism has ever produced, more durable than any format or platform or delivery mechanism.

Every session of this module opens with something like that conversation. Students arrive having followed their beat for another week, having written something by hand about what they noticed. They sit down with their classmates and talk about it. The five critical moves give the conversation structure. The notebook gives it memory. The physical publication gives it a form that lasts beyond the quarter.

That conversation is the antidote this curriculum is trying to build. Not a technology, not a platform, not an app. A practice of sitting down, reading carefully, writing by hand, and talking to another person about what you found. The hot take culture, the sensationalist headline, the AI-generated summary — none of those require you to slow down. This course does. That is the whole point.

On Technology and Determinism

This curriculum does not take the position that AI is destroying journalism or saving it. It takes the position that technology changes the conditions, and that human beings, individually and collectively, still determine what they do inside those conditions. That is not technological determinism, which holds that the technology writes the outcome. It is the more complicated and more honest position: the outcome is contested and made, not given.

That position has a practical implication for instruction. Students tend to arrive at this curriculum with one of two default views. Either technology is destiny (resistance is futile, just learn to use the tools), or technology is a choice (we could simply opt out, if we wanted to). Both are wrong in ways that matter for media literacy. The first produces fatalism. The second produces denial. The position this curriculum trains is the one in between: change the conditions does not mean write the outcome. Pay attention. Apply the moves. Contribute what only you can contribute. That is where the agency lives.

A note on the moment this was built

This curriculum was developed in spring 2026, at a moment when public attitudes toward AI were shifting measurably and fast. The specific controversies of that moment will have changed by the time you are reading this. The argument the curriculum makes does not depend on those controversies. It depends on a structural condition that will persist: AI is a force in the information environment. It is not the only force. Understanding how information is made, distributed, evaluated, and believed — and practicing that understanding deliberately — is what media literacy has always been about. The AI moment makes it more urgent. It does not make it different in kind.

Choosing Your Version

S / M / L Explained

Three versions of the same curriculum. The size describes the scope you choose for your course, not the physical format of any deliverable.

The Key Distinction

Small, Medium, and Large are not about how much content there is. They are about how much of the full critical process your course has room to practice. The Large version runs all five moves across ten weeks and produces a collective capstone. The Medium version runs all five moves across five weeks and produces an individual public piece. The Small version runs the first three moves in as little as one session and produces a reading reflection. Each contains the previous: a Small session inside a Medium course is the same practice, just compressed.

S · Small

Any course. Any discipline. Any level.

One to three sessions, or a recurring weekly ritual. Three critical moves: Description, Analysis, Interpretation.

  • Fits inside an existing course without restructuring anything
  • Works at grades 9 through 16
  • 15 minutes per session or 1–2 dedicated sessions
  • One notebook entry per session, one Reading Reflection, one Self-Assessment
  • Physical deliverable: one page or one spread
M · Medium

Any course with five weeks of available thread.

Five weeks, all five moves, one live news story followed from Description to Engagement.

  • Designed for other disciplines adapting the journalism framework
  • One story, followed weekly, five assignments building on each other
  • Notebook entries plus a Short Public Piece
  • Physical deliverable: four to six pages
  • No cluster formation — individual arc throughout
L · Large

A journalism course. One quarter of the grade.

Ten weeks, all five moves in three cycles, cluster formation, collective State of the World capstone.

  • Designed for journalism courses at the quarter or semester level
  • One beat followed for ten weeks
  • Notebook runs all ten weeks, assessed holistically
  • Clusters form at Week 5, produce the State of the World entry
  • Physical deliverable: 20 pages assembled at the assembly session

The Ten-Week Arc (Large)

For instructors considering the Large version, here is the full arc across all five modules:

Module 1
Description (Weeks 1–2)
Students choose a beat, describe what it says, and map its source architecture. Beat Source Map due.
Module 2
Analysis (Weeks 3–4)
Students track how coverage works and moves. AI footprint traced. Story Arc Analysis due.
Module 3
Interpretation (Weeks 5–6)
Students apply disciplinary knowledge. Clusters form around shared underlying questions. Interpretive Brief due.
Module 4
Evaluation (Weeks 7–8)
Students make evaluative arguments. Clusters plan their State of the World entry. Equity Assessment and Cluster Proposal due.
Module 5
Engagement (Weeks 9–10)
Clusters draft, peer review, revise. Assembly session in Week 10. State of the World entry and physical publication due.
The Physical Practice

The Analog Layer

Why the notebook is kept by hand, what the physical publication is for, and what instructors need to decide before Week 1.

Why It Exists

The analog layer is not arts and crafts. It is grounded in a well-established body of research on handwriting and embodied cognition, confirmed by a 2024 study from the Norwegian University of Science and Technology, showing that writing by hand engages the brain differently from typing. It slows the process of recording in a way that forces synthesis: you cannot write down everything, so you decide what matters. That decision is the same act as the critical process, practiced at smaller scale, every week.

There is also a pedagogical argument that goes beyond the research. The physical publication exists because the iPad failed to save the magazine in 2010 — not because tablets were technically insufficient, but because the economic and behavioral logic of the screen favored the feed over the magazine's form. The publication this course produces is a deliberate response to that outcome: a hand-assembled, room-specific object that exists because a specific group of people made it, at a specific moment, about what they were actually watching in the world. It is not designed to look like the publications that feed-based distribution replaced. It is designed to be something different: specific, physical, made.

The Three Decisions You Make Before Week 1

Every Canvas shell has three bracketed fields that you fill in before deploying it. These are the only instructor-specific decisions the analog layer requires. Everything else is specified in the curriculum.

Decision What to Consider Examples
What will you call the notebook? Choose a name that connects to your discipline's culture. The name signals what kind of practitioner you are training students to be. Reporter's notebook (journalism) · Field journal (sciences, social sciences) · Researcher's log (graduate courses) · Observer's record (humanities) · Sketchbook (arts and design)
What format will the publication take? Consider what your classroom can support. 11×17 requires access to a wide-format printer or students working at tabloid size. 8.5×11 is universal. Both work. 11×17 folded to tabloid (ideal for Large) · 8.5×11 booklet, saddle-stapled · Landscape broadsheet, unfolded · Any format the instructor specifies
What is the minimum page count? For Large: 20 pages is one per class session in a ten-week DU quarter. For Medium: 4–6 pages. For Small: one spread (4 sides of a folded sheet) is sufficient. Large: 20 pages · Medium: 4–6 pages · Small: one spread or four pages

The Notebook Specification

Any physical notebook with unlined or lightly lined pages works. Minimum 40 pages for Large, 20 for Medium, no minimum for Small beyond enough to work in. A three-dollar composition book with blank pages is as good as a twenty-dollar Leuchtturm. What matters is that it is physical, it is theirs, and it is kept by hand throughout the module.

Weekly entries follow a consistent three-part structure regardless of which module is active: What I noticed (one specific observation, not a summary), What moved (one thing that changed and what drove it), and What I'm sitting with (the question the student cannot yet answer, as specific as possible). Students photograph their entry and upload the photo to Canvas before class. The instructor reads the photos, not the handwriting. Assessment is holistic, at the end of the module, as an arc.

The Assembly Session

The assembly session is the final class meeting of the module for the Large version. It has three phases: assembly (approximately 30 minutes, clusters working at tables with their materials), reading aloud (approximately 15 minutes, one passage per cluster and one open question), and distribution (approximately 5 minutes, physical copies to the class, the opening read aloud). After distribution, the session ends.

Two things matter most about running the assembly well. First, protect the session: it is not a presentation, not a workshop, not a regular class. It is the thing itself. Students who miss it miss the culminating event of the module. Second, let the room be loud during assembly: the sound of people making things together in a physical space is exactly what the session is supposed to produce. Resist the impulse to quiet it.

The publication is submitted as photographs

After the assembly session, students photograph the complete publication with care and upload the image set to Canvas. The physical object is theirs to keep. The photograph set is the formal submission record. High-quality phone photos in good light are sufficient — a dedicated scan session produces better archival quality but is not required.

LMS Implementation

Canvas Setup

How to get the curriculum pages into your LMS, what to pin, what to name, and how the persistent threads work.

General Approach

Each Canvas shell file contains every page you need, organized in order. Open the file in a browser. Use the sidebar to navigate to each panel. At the top of every panel, a black bar marked "Canvas Page" tells you what the page is and what to name it in your LMS. Copy the content of the panel into a new Canvas page in your course. Repeat for each panel you want to deploy.

You do not need to deploy every page. The minimum viable deployment for each size is:

  • Small: Course Home, The Critical Process, and the Module 1 Overview. Everything else can be added as the module progresses.
  • Medium: Course Home, The Critical Process, The Slow News Practice, and all five Module Overview pages. Add assignment pages when each module opens.
  • Large: All six permanent pages plus Module 1 pages before Week 1. Add subsequent module pages as you open each module. Post the AI Thread before Week 1 and leave it open all term.

What to Pin

In Canvas, you can pin pages to the course home. For the Large version, pin these five pages in this order:

  1. Course Home / Welcome — the entry point for new students
  2. The Five Critical Moves — the permanent reference students return to all term
  3. The Slow News Practice — the notebook spec and publication guide
  4. The AI Observation Thread — the persistent discussion thread
  5. Reading List — the two-tier reading list for all ten weeks

For Small and Medium, pin Course Home and The Critical Process. Everything else can live in the module structure.

The AI Observation Thread

The AI Observation Thread is one discussion thread that runs across all ten weeks of the Large version. Create it before Week 1 and do not create a new one each week. Students add one observation any time they encounter AI's visible role in how a news item was generated, summarized, recommended, or distributed. One sentence on what they found; one sentence on how they knew or how they were not sure.

The thread's value accumulates over time. By Week 8 or 9, it contains a specific, varied, first-hand record of how AI is operating in the information environment the class navigates. The instructor reads it and brings observations from it into class discussion — not as a lesson, but as a contribution that the thread made possible. Point students back to it when reviewing for the State of the World entry.

Bracketed Fields to Fill In

Search your deployed Canvas pages for square brackets before the first session. Every bracketed field is an instructor decision. The main ones:

  • [Instructor fills in — reporter's notebook / field journal / researcher's log / observer's record] — the name of the notebook for your course
  • [Instructor fills in — 11x17 tabloid folded / 8.5x11 booklet / landscape broadsheet / other] — the publication format
  • [Instructor fills in — e.g., one spread per cluster plus a cover / four pages minimum / twenty pages for the full version] — the publication minimum
  • [X]% in the Syllabus — the percentage of the course grade this module represents

All other content is complete as written. You can adjust tone, add examples from your field, or modify assignment word counts — but the framework, the rubrics, and the discussion prompts are ready to deploy without changes.

For Non-Journalism Instructors

Adapting for Your Discipline

What journalism is the home base for, what stays the same in any field, and how to swap the examples without rebuilding the curriculum.

What Stays the Same

The critical process, all five moves of it, works in any discipline without modification. Description, Analysis, Interpretation, Evaluation, and Engagement are not journalism-specific operations. They are ways of reading carefully, tracing how things work, bringing knowledge to bear, making accountable judgments, and contributing to a public conversation. A biology professor and a journalism professor teach the same five moves. The beats their students follow are different. The framework is the same.

The following elements require no adaptation: the five moves themselves, the notebook structure and submission process, the physical publication format and assembly session, the discussion board structure and AI thread, the rubrics for all five major assignments, and the cluster formation process in the Large version. These are designed to work across any subject area.

What Changes by Discipline

Two things change when you adapt the curriculum for a field other than journalism. The first is the Tier 2 reading — the field example that shows AI operating in your discipline, calibrated to that module's critical move. The second is the entry point for the Interpretation move, which asks students to bring disciplinary knowledge to bear. In journalism courses, the disciplinary lens is press theory, platform economics, and editorial standards. In other fields, the lens is whatever your discipline's specific framework is for understanding what your beat is about.

Module Journalism example (home base) What to look for in your field
Module 1 · Description AP's public disclosure of how it uses automated journalism tools A leading institution in your field's public disclosure of AI use — a hospital, a law firm, a research university, a financial institution. Run Description on it: what does it say, what does it not say?
Module 2 · Analysis Nieman Lab's annual synthesis of how AI changed newsroom practice The annual industry AI adoption report for your field — McKinsey, Deloitte, or the relevant professional association's annual technology report
Module 3 · Interpretation ProPublica data journalism methodology note — the reporter's judgment made explicit A recent example from your field where expert interpretation of AI-assisted output changed the meaning of results — a researcher who identified what the model got wrong, a clinician who added context the diagnostic AI missed
Module 4 · Evaluation A local news AI case study where the equity stakes are concrete and named A specific AI equity case in your field with named affected parties, concrete stakes, and a contested outcome — not a general argument about AI and labor, but a specific case
Module 5 · Engagement A newsroom's public AI standards document — an accountability statement directed at a public audience A professional association's ethics statement or standards document about AI in your field — AMA, ABA, AIA, IEEE, SAG-AFTRA. Run Description and Evaluation on it.

The Interpretation Entry Point by Discipline

The Interpretation move asks students to complete this sentence: "What this beat is really about, when you understand [something from my field or training], is..." The bracketed phrase is the entry point, and it differs by discipline. Here are starting points by field. The specific version is always the instructor's and the student's to name:

  • Sciences and engineering: "When you understand the methodology of the underlying study..." or "When you understand what a p-value actually means and what it does not mean..."
  • Social sciences: "When you understand the structural conditions that produced this situation..." or "When you understand whose interests the policy is designed to serve..."
  • Humanities: "When you understand the historical precedent for this moment..." or "When you understand how this story is being rhetorically framed and what that framing excludes..."
  • Business, law, and policy: "When you understand how decisions like this are actually made inside institutions..." or "When you understand the economic incentive structure driving this outcome..."
  • Arts and design: "When you understand the formal conventions being invoked or broken here..." or "When you understand what this kind of coverage has historically done to subjects like this one..."
  • Health and medicine: "When you understand the difference between statistical significance and clinical significance..." or "When you understand how the research pipeline between laboratory and public actually works..."

The Beat in Non-Journalism Courses

Students in non-journalism courses sometimes struggle to identify a beat that is both live and connected to their discipline. The assignment works best when instructors offer one or two examples from their own field before asking students to choose. The beat criteria remain the same in any discipline: live, traceable to a primary source, connected to the student's training or experience. What changes is the pool of examples.

A reliable fallback for any discipline

If students are struggling to find a beat, offer this: follow how your field's research or practice is being covered by mainstream media right now. Every discipline has coverage — some of it accurate, some of it partial, some of it seriously wrong. Following that coverage and applying the critical process to it uses both the discipline knowledge the student already has and the media literacy the module is building. It is always live, always traceable, always connected to training, and almost always produces a meaningful Interpretive Brief.

Keeping It Current

Maintenance and Updates

What needs updating each term, what is stable indefinitely, and how long the annual update actually takes.

What Is Stable

The following elements of this curriculum are written to hold across time without updating. They do not depend on current events, current technology, or current poll numbers. They depend on enduring questions about how information is made and evaluated:

  • The five critical moves and their definitions
  • The notebook structure and three-part weekly prompt
  • The physical publication format, assembly session structure, and submission process
  • The rubrics for all five major assignments
  • The discussion board structure and AI thread format
  • The Tier 1 spine readings: Lippmann, Campbell, Kovach and Rosenstiel, Bell and Owen
  • The "A Note on the Moment" framing (written to acknowledge that its specific examples will date, while the argument holds)
  • The evolution of news page up through generative AI (the structural history, not the current controversy)
  • The thesis, the coffee klatch framing, and the two-layer structure argument

What Needs Annual Updating

These elements should be reviewed before each term. The update is typically light — one link check and one replacement reading per module. The total time for a full annual update is approximately two to three hours:

What the Annual Update Takes

In practice, the annual update for the Large version takes about two to three hours the first time and about one hour in subsequent years. The update is:

  1. Check five links (Reuters Institute, Edelman, Nieman Lab, AP disclosure, any Tier 2 examples that change)
  2. Update three annual publications to current year editions
  3. Replace the first paragraph of "A Note on the Moment" with current examples
  4. Scan the Tier 2 slot for each module and ask: is there a better current example from this field? Replace if yes.

The Small and Medium versions require the same link checks but fewer replacements, since they use fewer total readings. Both can be updated in under an hour.

The simplest maintenance practice

Keep a running note in your course files with the URLs for the five annual publications and the date you last checked them. At the start of each term, spend thirty minutes on link verification before you deploy the Canvas pages. That thirty minutes protects the whole term from broken links and outdated data.

If you teach the Medium or Small version in a field other than journalism and develop strong Tier 2 examples for your discipline, consider sharing those examples so instructors in your field can benefit from your work. The goal of the project is reuse and adaptation, not proprietary control.

For questions about the curriculum, adaptations, or the long-term project, contact the Department of Media, Film and Journalism Studies at the University of Denver.

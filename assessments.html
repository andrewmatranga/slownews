Assignments, Assessments, and Rubrics

The complete assessment system for all three versions — prompts, rubrics with level descriptors, exemplar language, and instructor guidance.

How to Use This File

This file is the assessment companion to the Slow News, Smart Citizens Canvas shells. Every assignment in every version of the curriculum is documented here with a full student-facing prompt, a rubric with level descriptors (not just point values), exemplar language for strong and weak work, and instructor guidance on the most common errors and how to address them in feedback.

The Canvas shell pages contain abbreviated rubrics suitable for Canvas's built-in rubric tool. This file contains the full versions — everything you need to assess fairly and provide feedback that students can use.

Three versions, one assessment philosophy

The same critical process underlies all three versions. The assessment criteria scale with scope, not with standard. A Small version Reading Reflection and a Large version Interpretive Brief are both asking whether the student did the move with specificity and genuine intellectual engagement. The level descriptors use the same language across all three versions so students who move from Small to Medium to Large encounter a consistent framework.

The Assessment Philosophy

Every rubric in this system is built around the same underlying question: did the student do the specific thing this move requires, or did they do something adjacent to it? The most common failure mode in media literacy assessment is accepting a sophisticated-sounding general claim in place of a specific, grounded one. The rubrics are designed to catch that substitution and reward the genuine move.

  • Description is assessed for accuracy and specificity, not length. A student who accurately describes in two sentences what a story says has done Description. A student who spends four paragraphs paraphrasing the article's argument without accounting for what its evidence actually claims has not.
  • Analysis is assessed for structural specificity. Not "AI shapes coverage" but "the AI summary in Google's search preview for this story omitted the primary source's qualification in paragraph four." The less general the observation, the better the Analysis.
  • Interpretation is assessed against the substitution test: could someone without this student's training, field, and weeks of following this beat have written this? If yes, the student has described rather than interpreted. Send it back.
  • Evaluation is assessed for the counterargument. An evaluation that does not engage the strongest version of the opposing position is advocacy, not evaluation. The rubric weights this heavily.
  • Engagement is assessed for contribution. Does the writing say something a reader who followed this beat for ten weeks needed to know? If it could have been written without the beat work, the Engagement move has not been made.

Level Descriptors

Every rubric in this file uses five performance levels. The labels and their meanings are consistent across all assignments and all versions:

Excellent
Full credit
Does the specific move this assignment requires with precision, specificity, and genuine intellectual engagement. Exceeds the prompt's expectations without being asked to. The work could not have been produced without this student's particular training and sustained attention.
Proficient
85–90% of element points
Does the move correctly and with adequate specificity. Meets all elements of the prompt. The work is solid and reliable. It does not exceed expectations but it fulfills them. Minor imprecisions in language or framing that do not distort the core move.
Developing
70–80% of element points
Attempts the move but substitutes something adjacent. A general claim where a specific one is required. A description that slides into evaluation. An interpretive claim that a general reader could have made without the student's training. The instinct is right; the execution is not yet.
Beginning
50–65% of element points
The move is attempted but significantly misunderstood or replaced with an easier one. Summary in place of Description. Reaction in place of Evaluation. Generic observation in place of Interpretation. The assignment is complete but the critical move is not made.
Insufficient
0–40% of element points
The element is missing, too brief to assess, or demonstrates fundamental misunderstanding of the assignment. Not a quality judgment — an absence. Return with a specific note on what is missing and a deadline for resubmission where the course policy allows it.

On the holistic assessments

The Notebook Archive and the Self-Assessment are not separately scored. They are submitted with the Module 5 package and contribute to the holistic assessment of that submission. A student who submits strong written work but a perfunctory or incomplete notebook will find that reflected in the Module 5 score. Both matter. Neither has a separate point value because neither is a separate document — they are evidence of the practice that makes the written work possible.

Grading Philosophy for the Large Version

Each of the five major assignments represents one move in the critical process. The assignments build on each other: the Beat Source Map in Week 2 is the foundation the Interpretive Brief in Week 6 depends on. A student who does perfunctory work in Week 2 will find the Week 6 assignment harder to write well — not as a punishment, but as a structural consequence of the move sequence.

Return written feedback on each assignment within one week of the due date. The most important feedback moment is the Interpretive Brief: students need to know whether their underlying question is strong enough to seed a cluster argument before they begin Module 4. Brief feedback on the underlying question alone — is it specific enough? does it genuinely require disciplinary knowledge to ask? — is more valuable than comprehensive comments on every element.

Module 1 · Description · Due end of Week 2

Beat Source Map

A map of where your beat's news comes from, how it travels, and where AI enters the chain. The document that makes everything else in this module possible.

Length 400–500 words
Format Typed prose, submitted to Canvas
Points 100
Analog companion Hand-drawn source map sketch in notebook (not submitted separately)

The Prompt (Student-Facing)

The Beat Source Map establishes the information architecture of your chosen beat. It is not a summary of the news. It is a map of where the news comes from, how it travels to a general audience, and where AI enters that journey. A careful map now makes every subsequent assignment in this module significantly easier to write with depth and specificity.

Before you write the typed submission, sketch the source architecture by hand in your notebook: draw the three layers, draw where AI enters, draw the path a story takes from primary source to your screen. The sketch is preparation, not a submission. It often produces insights the written version then captures.

Your submission addresses five elements in prose, 400 to 500 words:

  1. Name the beat and the thread. One sentence each. The beat is the ongoing field. The thread is the specific developing story you are following right now. Present tense. Specific. Traceable to a primary source.
  2. Map the three source layers. Primary source: who or what generates the original material — named institutions, documents, actors, events. Specialist layer: who covers it with expertise. Mainstream layer: where the general public encounters it. Name specific examples at each layer.
  3. Trace the AI footprint. Where does AI enter your beat's information system — in recommendation, summarization, generation, or distribution? At least one specific, concrete observation about what AI does to your beat's coverage. Not a general statement about AI in news. Something specific to what you observed this week in this beat.
  4. Name your disciplinary angle. One sentence connecting your field or background to this beat — specific enough to seed the Interpretive Brief in Module 3. Not "I study journalism" but "my training in [specific area] lets me see [specific thing] in this beat."
  5. Name one structural pattern you already see. Something about how this beat's coverage works that you noticed in the first two weeks. Descriptive, not evaluative. An observation about structure, not a judgment about quality.

What Strong Work Looks Like

Exemplar — Beat and Thread (Excellent)

"My beat is pharmaceutical pricing policy in the United States. My thread is the Senate Finance Committee's ongoing investigation into insulin pricing at three major manufacturers, which filed preliminary findings in late September. The primary source is the committee's public document repository at finance.senate.gov."

Exemplar — AI Footprint (Excellent)

"When I searched 'insulin pricing Senate investigation' on Monday, Google's AI Overview synthesized an answer that cited the investigation's findings but attributed them to the manufacturers rather than to the committee — reversing the accountability direction of the original document. The mainstream article the Overview linked to did not make this error. The AI summary did."

Exemplar — Disciplinary Angle (Excellent)

"My training in health policy journalism lets me see that this story is structured around a document-release cycle — the committee drops filings on a schedule that sets the media's agenda — rather than around the underlying policy question, which has not changed in a decade."

Common Errors and How to Address Them

Common Errors in the Beat Source Map
  • The topic trap. Students name a topic ("healthcare," "climate," "AI") rather than a beat with a thread. A topic has no primary source and generates no weekly news. Redirect: what specific institution, document, or actor is generating new material every week? That is the beat.
  • The AI generality. "AI shapes how I find news" is not an AI footprint. An AI footprint is a specific instance: this summary, this recommendation, this generated paragraph, observed this week in this beat. Ask students to name the specific platform, the specific article, and the specific thing AI did or did not do accurately.
  • The discipline placeholder. "My background in journalism lets me evaluate sources" applies to every journalism student following every beat. The disciplinary angle must be specific to the beat: what does training in this area of the discipline let this student see in this specific story that a general reader cannot?
  • The structural evaluation. Students frequently write "the coverage is shallow" or "the coverage ignores marginalized voices" and present it as a structural observation. These are evaluative claims — not wrong, but belonging in Module 4, not Module 1. Redirect: describe the structure first. What is the coverage doing? Then evaluate what it should be doing.

Module 2 · Analysis · Due end of Week 4

Story Arc Analysis

An analytical argument about how your beat's coverage has worked across four weeks. Not what the story is about — how the coverage functions as a system.

Length 400–500 words
Format Typed prose
Points 100
Draws from Beat Source Map + four weeks of notebook entries

The Prompt (Student-Facing)

The Story Arc Analysis asks you to make an argument about structure — not about the events your beat covers, but about how the coverage of those events functions. How did coverage develop over four weeks? What drove the turning points? Who spoke and who was absent? What does the AI footprint look like across four weeks rather than one? The central deliverable is one arguable analytical claim, supported by specific evidence from your beat work.

Your submission addresses four elements in prose, 400 to 500 words:

  1. The arc. Map the development of your thread across four weeks of coverage. Name the key turning points and what drove them — new evidence, new sources, editorial decisions, or shifts in how the algorithm surfaces the story. Four or more specific coverage items, with dates.
  2. The voices. Who has spoken in your beat's coverage across four weeks, named specifically? Who has been described but not directly heard? Who does not appear at all? Propose a structural explanation for the pattern — not "the media ignored X" but a specific reason why the information architecture of your beat produces this voice distribution.
  3. The AI footprint. Where has AI touched your beat's coverage over four weeks — in how stories were found, summarized, distributed, or generated? At least two specific observations about what AI did, not one general statement about what AI does.
  4. The analytical claim. Two to three sentences stating your argument about how this coverage works — a claim about structure, not a judgment about quality. Specific enough that someone who followed your beat could meaningfully disagree with it on the basis of evidence.

Turning points described generally ("coverage increased after the announcement") without structural explanation. Fewer than two coverage items. Shows week-over-week pattern, not just two isolated instances. Two specific observations present. May not show a pattern but both are concrete and firsthand. One specific observation and one general claim. Or two observations that are essentially the same observation repeated in different weeks. General claim about AI in news applied to the beat. What Strong Work Looks Like

Exemplar — Analytical Claim (Excellent)

"Coverage of the insulin pricing investigation follows a reactive arc: the committee releases a document, mainstream outlets publish a story within 24 hours that paraphrases the committee's framing without reaching the manufacturers for comment, and the story disappears within 72 hours until the next document release. The investigation has been running for eight weeks; no outlet has produced an explanatory piece situating the findings in the decade-long legislative history of pharmaceutical pricing policy."

Exemplar — Voice Inventory (Excellent)

"Named sources across four weeks: Committee Chair [name], three patient advocacy organizations, two health economists (both affiliated with think tanks that receive pharmaceutical industry funding — not disclosed in coverage). Named and present in documents but never quoted: the manufacturers' responses to the committee's preliminary findings, which are in the public record. Absent entirely: patients, pharmacists, or anyone whose daily experience is shaped by the pricing practices under investigation. The structural reason is access: journalists work from the press release cycle the committee controls, and the manufacturers' responses are in document format that requires reading rather than quoting."

Common Errors
  • The topic summary. Students describe what the underlying story is about across four weeks rather than how coverage of it worked. The arc should describe the coverage, not the events. Redirect: not "the committee released three documents" but "coverage spiked within 24 hours of each document release and returned to baseline within 72 hours — suggesting a release-driven rather than story-driven coverage pattern."
  • The general AI claim. "AI summarized my beat's stories" is not a footprint. Ask students to name one specific AI summary they encountered, what it said, and how it differed from or matched the underlying article. The specificity is the analysis.
  • The evaluative analytical claim. The most common error in Module 2 is sliding from "how does coverage work" to "is the coverage adequate." The analytical claim should describe a structural pattern, not judge it. Judgment comes in Module 4. Send evaluative claims back with a note: "This is your Module 4 claim. What structural pattern produced the situation you are evaluating?"

Module 3 · Interpretation · Due end of Week 6

Interpretive Brief

What your knowledge reveals that the coverage cannot say. The intellectual center of the module and the seed of your cluster's collective argument.

Length 500–650 words
Format Typed prose
Points 100
Instructor note Return with feedback before Module 5 begins

The Prompt (Student-Facing)

The Interpretive Brief is the intellectual center of the ten-week module. It asks you to say what your beat means in light of what you know — in a way the coverage itself cannot. It is not a summary of your analysis, not a reaction to the events, and not a preview of your evaluation. It is perception made precise: what a careful, educated person sees when they look at this beat that a casual reader does not.

The test for a genuine Interpretive Brief: could someone without your training, your field, and your six weeks of following this beat have written this? If the answer is yes, go further. The Brief earns its place when it says something that is only possible because of what you bring.

Your submission addresses four elements in prose, 500 to 650 words:

  1. What the coverage says the beat is about. One paragraph. Accurately describe what mainstream coverage presents as the beat's meaning. Use Description: what does the coverage actually say, not what you think of it?
  2. What your knowledge reveals. One to two paragraphs. Name the concept, framework, or field-specific understanding that changes how this beat reads. Explain it accessibly enough that a general reader can follow the logic. Then say specifically what the coverage misses or misrepresents that your training allows you to see.
  3. Your interpretive claim. One paragraph. What does this beat actually mean, in light of what you know? Specific, arguable, and only possible because of what you bring to it.
  4. The underlying question. One sentence. The question your interpretation opens but does not close — specific enough to seed your cluster's shared inquiry in Modules 4 and 5.

Account that slides into evaluation or interpretation before establishing what the coverage actually says. Or an account so general it could describe any story in this field. Missing or so brief it cannot be assessed. Or fundamentally inaccurate account of the coverage's framing. 15

May be explained with some jargon or applied slightly loosely but genuinely changes the reading. Disciplinary background described rather than applied. "My training in health policy" without showing what that training sees. Or knowledge applied so broadly it would illuminate any story in any field. No disciplinary knowledge applied. What Strong Work Looks Like

Exemplar — Disciplinary Knowledge Applied (Excellent)

"Health policy journalism has a concept for what is happening in this coverage: the therapeutic misconception, originally identified in clinical trial research, describes how people systematically overestimate the personal benefit and underestimate the systemic nature of medical interventions. The Senate investigation is being covered as a story about individual patients' insulin costs — and it is that — but the policy mechanism under investigation is a rebate system that operates at the payer level, not the patient level. Coverage that centers individual patient stories is not wrong; it is using the available emotional logic to tell a story whose actual mechanics are institutional. My training lets me see that the coverage is choosing the right emotional register for a story whose policy register is being systematically underrepresented."

Exemplar — Underlying Question (Excellent)

"When policy coverage systematically centers the emotional register of individual harm over the institutional mechanics of institutional decision-making, who decides which register is primary — and what does that choice do to public understanding of the policy options available?"

Common Errors
  • The background problem. Students describe their training rather than apply it. "As a health policy student, I know that pharmaceutical pricing is complex" is background, not interpretation. What does health policy training let this student see in this specific beat that changes how it reads?
  • The general claim. "This story is really about power" or "this story is really about inequality" fails the substitution test. Any attentive reader can make that claim. What specifically — what mechanism, what institutional structure, what disciplinary concept — is producing the coverage's specific form?
  • The premature evaluation. The Interpretive Brief is not the Equity Assessment. Students who arrive at "the coverage is inadequate because..." have skipped Interpretation and moved to Evaluation. Redirect: what does your training let you see about how the coverage works — before you judge whether it works well?
  • The question that closes. "Is the coverage adequate?" is not a good underlying question — it can be answered with the Equity Assessment. A good underlying question cannot be answered by one student in one module. It requires the cluster's collective attention from multiple angles.

Module 4 · Evaluation · Due end of Week 8

Equity Assessment and Cluster Proposal

Two deliverables due together. The Equity Assessment closes your individual arc. The Cluster Proposal opens the collective one.

Equity Assessment Individual · 500–600 words · 100 points
Cluster Proposal Group · 300–400 words · Completion-graded
Due End of Week 8

Part 1: Equity Assessment — The Prompt (Student-Facing)

The Equity Assessment is your structured evaluative argument about your beat. It asks you to move beyond description, analysis, and interpretation to make a reasoned judgment: about what is at stake for real people, whether the distribution of costs and benefits is fair, and whether the coverage adequately serves public understanding of those stakes.

Evaluation is not reaction. A reaction is what you felt when you first read the headline at Week 1. An evaluation is what you can say after eight weeks of careful attention: a claim grounded in evidence, named by a principle you can state and defend, and specific about who is affected and how.

Your submission addresses four elements in prose, 500 to 600 words:

  1. The stakes. Who is affected by the developments in your beat? Name them specifically — not a category, but named communities, institutions, or people. What do they stand to gain or lose?
  2. The evaluative claim. Is the distribution of costs and benefits in your beat fair or unfair, according to a principle you can name and defend? Support your claim with specific evidence from your eight weeks of beat work.
  3. Coverage evaluation. Does your beat's coverage adequately represent the equity and ethical dimensions you have identified? What is missing, and why — structurally — might it be absent?
  4. The counterargument. State the strongest objection to your evaluation and respond to it substantively — not by dismissing it, but by engaging with it and saying what it adds or changes to your position.

The reader understands why this matters to specific people. Specific affected parties named. Stakes present. May be slightly general about what specifically is at risk for each party. Categories rather than named parties ("patients," "communities," "workers"). Stakes described generally without grounding in specific beat developments. No specific affected parties. Stakes described at the level of the topic ("healthcare matters to everyone") rather than the beat. 20

The claim is supported by specific evidence from the beat — not from general knowledge about the topic. Could be meaningfully disputed by someone who disagrees with the principle or the evidence. Evaluative claim and principle present. Evidence offered. Principle may be implicit rather than fully named. Evaluative claim present but grounded in general topic knowledge rather than beat-specific evidence. Or principle vague ("fairness," "justice") without being made specific to this situation. Part 2: Cluster Proposal — The Prompt (Student-Facing)

One submission per cluster. Graded on completion and specificity. A Proposal that addresses all four elements with genuine specificity receives full credit.

  1. The shared question. One sentence — specific enough to guide the State of the World entry and broad enough to genuinely require all beats in the cluster to address it.
  2. What each beat contributes. One sentence per beat on the irreplaceable thing it brings to the cluster argument — the angle, the knowledge, or the evidence that only that thread can supply.
  3. Proposed content. What will a reader encounter in the State of the World entry? What will they understand after reading it that they did not understand before?
  4. Publication plan. What pages in the physical publication will this cluster produce? What form will they take?
Cluster Proposal grading

The Cluster Proposal is graded on completion and specificity, not on quality of the proposed argument — that is what Module 5 is for. A Proposal that genuinely addresses all four elements with specificity receives full credit. A Proposal that is vague, incomplete, or that could describe any cluster in the class receives partial credit proportional to how much of the specificity is present. Return Proposals that use placeholder language ("we will write about our beats") with a note asking for the specific version.

What Strong Work Looks Like

Exemplar — Evaluative Claim (Excellent)

"The Senate investigation's findings document a pattern in which a rebate system created by the Affordable Care Act, intended to reduce patient costs, was instead used by pharmacy benefit managers to extract value from manufacturers without passing savings to patients. The principle I am applying is distributive justice: that policy mechanisms should benefit the populations they are designed to serve, and that when they do not, accountability requires naming who captured the benefit instead. The evidence from eight weeks of beat work shows that mainstream coverage has consistently framed the investigation as a story about manufacturer greed rather than as a story about how a policy mechanism was captured by intermediaries — which is a materially different claim with materially different policy implications."

Common Errors
  • The category stakeholder. "Patients" is not a specific affected party. "The estimated 8.4 million Americans who ration insulin due to cost" is. The specificity of the stakeholder account signals whether the student has been following an actual beat or a topic.
  • The reaction-as-evaluation. "This is deeply unfair" is a reaction. An evaluation names a principle: unfair by what standard, compared to what alternative, and in what specific way? Without the named principle, the evaluative claim cannot be disputed — and undisputable claims are not arguments.
  • The weak counterargument. "Some might say the coverage is fine, but I disagree" is not a counterargument. Ask students: what is the strongest case that your evaluative claim is wrong? What evidence would a reasonable person point to? Only after engaging that evidence can the student's position be defended rather than merely asserted.

Module 5 · Engagement · Due at Week 10 Assembly

State of the World Entry and Physical Publication

The piece of writing that exists because this specific group of people followed these specific beats at this specific moment and then looked at what they found together.

Entry length 500–700 words per cluster
Format Typed prose (cluster submission) + physical publication (assembled at assembly session)
Points 100 (combined with notebook archive and self-assessment)
Process Draft → peer review → revision → assembly

The Prompt (Student-Facing)

The State of the World entry is the piece of writing that exists because this specific group of people followed these specific beats at this specific moment and then looked at what they found together. It is not a collection of individual summaries. It is a collective argument — one that requires all of the cluster's beats to sustain, and that could not have been made by any one person alone.

Before submitting, apply the test: could a reader who has never heard of any of these beats encounter this entry and find it worth reading? Does it make a claim rather than describe a situation? Does it draw on the evidence of all the beats in the cluster? Does it say something that ten weeks of collective attention made possible? If the answer to any of these is no, revise before submitting.

The process: draft submitted by mid-Week 9, peer review returned by end of Week 9, revised entry submitted before the assembly session in Week 10.

Physical Publication

The physical publication is assembled during the Week 10 assembly session. What "assembled" means

The publication is assembled by hand in the room. Typed text is printed and physically placed on the artboard. Hand-lettered headlines, annotations, and drawings are made directly on the page. The seams and imperfections are not flaws. They are evidence that specific people made it. A cluster that produces a polished digital-looking document with all text pre-formatted has not done the assignment. A cluster whose pages look made — uneven, annotated, visibly assembled — has.

Peer Review

An editorial response to another cluster's State of the World draft. Not praise, not criticism — an editorial question that moves the entry forward.

Length 200–300 words
Format Group submission
Assessment Completion-graded — present and substantive = full credit
Due End of Week 9

The Prompt (Student-Facing)

Your cluster has been assigned one other cluster's draft State of the World entry to review. Peer review is not praise and it is not criticism — it is an editorial response. The goal is to help the entry say what it is trying to say more clearly and more completely before the assembly session.

Your review addresses three things:

Description — What does this entry actually say?

State the central claim in your own words. Not what the entry is trying to say — what it actually says on the page. If you cannot state the claim clearly in one sentence, the entry has a Description problem: its central argument is not yet visible to a reader coming to it fresh.

Analysis — Where does the synthesis feel genuine?

Where does the entry say something that requires all of the beats to sustain? Name it specifically. Then: where does it feel like separate pieces sitting next to each other rather than a genuine collective argument? Name that specifically too.

Editorial Question — What is the one thing this entry needs?

Ask it as a question, not a directive. Not "you need to add more evidence" but "what would the reader need to know about the relationship between [X] and [Y] for the claim in paragraph three to hold?" One question. The most important one. Leave the revision to the cluster.

Completion Assessment

Peer review is completion-graded. A review that addresses all three elements with genuine specificity, not praise (not "it was interesting"), but specific observations about what the entry says and what it needs) — receives full credit. A review that is vague, formulaic, or clearly not engaged with the specific draft receives partial credit proportional to how much genuine engagement is present.

For instructors — on monitoring peer review quality

Read the peer reviews before returning them to clusters. A review that says "the entry is well-written and covers all the beats" has not done the editorial work — it has offered praise. Return it with a note asking for the Description (what does the entry actually argue?) and the editorial question (what does it need to argue it better?). The quality of peer review is often a leading indicator of the quality of the final entry: clusters that engage genuinely with each other's drafts produce stronger State of the World entries.

What Strong Work Looks Like

Exemplar — Description (Excellent)

"The article claims that a recent study found a correlation between adolescent social media use and increased rates of self-reported anxiety. The primary evidence cited is a survey of 3,200 teenagers conducted by a university research group. The headline says 'Social media causes teen anxiety'; the article's own methodology section notes the study is correlational and cannot establish causation. That gap between headline and body is the most important thing in the piece."

Exemplar — Interpretation (Excellent)

"My coursework in research methods lets me see that the study this article is based on has a significant self-report problem: it asks teenagers to estimate their own social media use and their own anxiety levels, both of which are notoriously unreliable self-assessments. The study's authors acknowledge this in the limitations section. The article does not. My training tells me to look at the methodology before accepting the headline's causal claim — and the methodology here does not support it."

Common Errors
  • Description that evaluates. "The story says X, which is misleading" has already left Description and entered Evaluation. The Description move accounts for what the story says before judging whether it says it accurately. Split the moves: say what is there, then analyze how it got there, then interpret what your knowledge adds.
  • Analysis that stays on the surface. "I found this on Twitter" is not Analysis. Analysis traces the path: what was the primary source, how did it become a story, how did AI touch it along the way? The student who says "I found it because an algorithm surfaced it in my feed, and the AI summary omitted the study's methodology section" has done Analysis.
  • Interpretation as opinion. "In my opinion, social media is harmful" is not Interpretation in the critical process sense. Interpretation is what your training, your field, or your specific knowledge reveals about this story. The discipline is the lens; the story is what the lens illuminates.

2. Analysis

Where did you notice AI's role in how a news item reached you — either in this module or in the week after it ended? Name what you found and how you knew (or how you were not sure).

3. Interpretation

What is one thing your field, your training, or your experience lets you see in a news story that a general reader would miss? Try to be specific — not "my background helps me read critically" but a particular thing your particular preparation reveals.

Completion Assessment

All three questions answered with genuine specificity (not placeholder language, not "I learned to think critically") receive full credit. The self-assessment is not separately scored in the Small version's 100-point total, but it is a required submission. Students who do not submit it have not completed the module.

Using self-assessments for course improvement

The Small version self-assessment is the most useful feedback instrument in this file. Students who answer question 2 specifically — naming what they found and how they noticed it — are giving you a record of how AI is actually operating in the information environment they navigate. Read them. They tell you which moves stuck, where the AI conversation landed, and what students are actually noticing in their media diet. This is more useful data than an end-of-course survey.

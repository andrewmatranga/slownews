Concept Overview
Trust and Authority in the AI Era
Who gets to be believed, how that is being renegotiated, and what journalism has always had to say about it.

This Is Not a New Problem

Journalism has always been in the business of credibility. The question of why anyone should believe a news report — rather than the person next to them, the pamphlet on the street corner, or the rumor circulating at work — is as old as the press itself. What changes across time is not the question but the answer: which institutions get trusted, on what grounds, and what it takes to lose that trust.

In the twentieth century, trust in journalism was largely institutional. You believed a report in part because of which newspaper carried it, which network broadcast it, which wire service filed it. That institutional trust was imperfect — it encoded assumptions about whose voice counted, whose experience was news, whose community warranted coverage. But it provided a shared reference point: a space where claims could be checked against a relatively legible set of standards.

That institutional framework has been eroding for decades, accelerated by the internet, social media, partisan media ecosystems, and now AI. The question of who gets to be believed has become both more urgent and more genuinely difficult to answer.

"Authority is not the same as accuracy. Credibility is not the same as truth. But in a functioning information environment, they need to be connected — and right now, that connection is under serious strain."

What AI Changes About Authority

AI introduces at least three distinct challenges to the existing frameworks of journalistic authority — and they are worth keeping separate, because conflating them produces confusion rather than clarity.

The Authorship Problem

When text is generated by a large language model, the question of who is responsible for its claims becomes genuinely complicated. There is no reporter who made a judgment call about what to include. There is no editor who checked it against a standard. There is an output that may be accurate, inaccurate, or somewhere in between — with no clear accountability structure attached to it. Authorship has always been a proxy for accountability. AI severs that connection.

The Provenance Problem

Generative AI search synthesizes answers from sources it does not fully disclose. Even when citations are provided, the relationship between the citation and the claim may be weak or misrepresented. The reader cannot trace the chain from answer to evidence in the way they can with a traditional reported article. This does not mean the answer is wrong — but it removes the reader's ability to evaluate it independently.

The Volume Problem

When the cost of producing plausible content approaches zero, the information environment fills with it. Not because anyone intends harm — though sometimes they do — but because the economic incentives for producing content at scale no longer require the friction that quality journalism has always depended on. Verification takes time. Sourcing takes relationships. Editing takes judgment. These are costs that AI-generated content does not have to bear.

The Backlash Problem

By spring 2026, public trust in AI had declined sharply and rapidly — faster than pollsters had seen with any comparable technology. This matters for journalism because AI tools are now embedded in newsroom workflows, search products, and content distribution. A public that distrusts AI may extend that distrust to journalism that uses it, accurately or not. The credibility of the tool and the credibility of the institution are becoming entangled, whether journalists want them to be or not.

What Journalism Has Always Known

None of this makes the traditional journalistic answers to the authority question obsolete — it makes them more important. The practices that have always grounded credible journalism are exactly the practices that AI-generated content tends to lack: named sources, verifiable documents, transparent methodology, editorial accountability, correction culture, and the reporter's judgment about what matters and why.

These are not romantic attachments to an idealized past. They are functional responses to the problem of credibility. They exist because readers have always needed ways to evaluate whether to trust a claim — and journalism developed specific tools for making that evaluation possible. Those tools are worth understanding, defending, and practicing, especially in a moment when they are under pressure.

For journalism students specifically

Your authority as a journalist will not come from the institution you work for — at least not primarily. It will come from the quality of your sourcing, the transparency of your methods, the accuracy of your record, and your willingness to correct errors. Those are things an AI cannot fake and that a reader can actually evaluate. That is the ground you stand on.

What This Means for the Critical Process

The Analysis move — asking where a story came from and how it reached you — is fundamentally a question about authority. Who is making this claim? On what grounds? What accountability structure do they operate within? What happens if they are wrong?

The Interpretation move — bringing your own knowledge to bear — is an act of authority. You are not just receiving a claim; you are evaluating it against what you know. That evaluation is more valuable, not less, in an environment where authority is harder to establish from source alone.

The Engagement move — saying something in public, for a named audience — is an exercise of authority. It comes with responsibility. The Short Public Piece and the State of the World entry are not just assignments. They are practice for being the kind of voice the information environment actually needs: sourced, accountable, specific, and honest about what you do not know.

A Note on the Moment

This curriculum was developed in spring 2026, at a moment when public attitudes toward AI shifted measurably and fast. Pollsters described the speed of the shift as unprecedented. By the time students are reading this, things may look different — the technology will have changed, the cultural conversation will have moved, the specific controversies that were live in May 2026 will have been replaced by new ones.

The questions this page is asking do not depend on the mood of any particular moment. They depend on paying attention to a structural condition that will persist regardless of whether AI is popular or unpopular in any given news cycle: the problem of who gets to be believed, and why, and what that means for the people doing journalism.

Concept Overview
AI in the Field — What It Actually Does, and What That Asks of You
Written from journalism. The swap-out structure is explicit — if you teach a different field, the examples change; the questions do not.

The Range Is Wider Than the Debate Suggests

The public conversation about AI in journalism tends to collapse into two positions: AI is destroying journalism, or AI is saving journalism. Both are wrong in the same way — they treat a set of specific, varied tools as a single phenomenon and then argue about the phenomenon rather than the tools.

AI is doing several distinct things in journalism right now, and they are worth keeping separate. Some are straightforward applications of automation to routine tasks. Some are genuine augmentations of investigative capacity. Some are serious threats to editorial integrity. And some are still being worked out by reporters, editors, and news organizations that are genuinely trying to figure out what responsible use looks like in practice.

This page introduces the range. The reading list for each week gives you a current example. Your job — in this course and after it — is to apply the critical process to each specific application rather than to the category.

If you teach a field other than journalism

This page is written from journalism, but the structure applies everywhere. AI is doing distinct things in your field too — some routine automation, some genuine augmentation, some genuine threat, some genuinely unresolved. The examples below are journalism examples. The questions at the end of each section are universal. Find the equivalent applications in your field and run the same questions on them.

Automation of Routine Content — What It Is and What It Is Not

The Associated Press has used automated writing tools to produce earnings reports and sports scores since 2014. Reuters has used similar tools for financial data. Local news organizations have experimented with AI-generated summaries of city council meetings, court filings, and weather alerts. This is a real and growing use of AI in journalism — and it is worth understanding clearly.

What automation does well: structured data converted to structured prose, at scale, faster than any human team could manage. Quarterly earnings for thousands of companies. Box scores for every minor league game. These are not stories — they are records, and AI can produce them more reliably and cheaply than humans can.

What automation does not do: judgment. Source selection. Context. The recognition that the numbers in this quarter's earnings report tell a different story if you know what happened to the company's workforce in the same period. Automated content handles the surface. Journalism handles everything underneath it.

The critical process question for automation

When you encounter a piece of content that may have been automated — a sports recap, a financial summary, a weather report — the Description move asks: what does it actually say? The Analysis move asks: who or what produced this, and what are the structural limits of that production method? What did the automation not have access to that a human reporter would?

Swap-out · If you teach business or finance

AI is automating earnings analysis, risk assessment summaries, and regulatory filing digests at financial firms. The same questions apply: what does the automated output handle well, what does it miss, and what does a human analyst bring that the tool cannot?

Swap-out · If you teach design or creative industries

AI is automating layout variations, image resizing, copy adaptation for different platforms, and A/B testing at scale. The questions are the same: what does the automation optimize for, what does it not have access to, and where does human judgment remain irreplaceable?

Augmentation of Investigative Work — The More Interesting Cases

Some of the most significant uses of AI in journalism are not in content production at all — they are in reporting. AI tools have been used to analyze hundreds of thousands of documents in public records investigations, to identify patterns in large datasets that no human team could process manually, and to surface sources and connections in complex financial networks.

ProPublica, The New York Times, and other investigative outlets have used machine learning to analyze data at scales that were previously impossible — satellite imagery, court filings, property records, campaign finance data. These are not stories AI wrote. They are stories AI made possible by handling the computational work that freed reporters to do the interpretive and relational work that only humans can do.

This is a genuinely different category from content automation, and it matters for how journalism students think about AI in their field. The tool is not replacing the reporter's judgment — it is expanding the reporter's capacity to apply that judgment to more evidence than any individual could previously access.

The critical process question for augmentation

When a story is based on AI-assisted data analysis, the Analysis move asks: what did the AI do, and what did the reporter do? What decisions were made by the tool, and what decisions were made by a human who is accountable for them? Transparency about method is part of what makes investigative journalism credible.

Swap-out · If you teach sciences or social sciences

AI is augmenting research in every empirical field — literature review, pattern detection in large datasets, image analysis in medical imaging and remote sensing. The same distinction applies: augmentation of human capacity versus replacement of human judgment. That line is where the interesting questions live.

Synthetic Media and Editorial Integrity

AI-generated images, audio, and video have introduced a category of threat to journalism that is genuinely new in kind, even if misinformation is not new. The question is no longer just whether a claim is true — it is whether the evidence presented in support of it is real. A photograph used to be presumptive evidence that something happened. That presumption is no longer reliable.

News organizations are developing standards for disclosure, verification, and the use of AI-generated imagery. Some are prohibiting it entirely in news contexts. Some are using it with disclosure for illustration. The standards are still being written, and they vary significantly across organizations and contexts.

This matters for journalism students not because they will inevitably be producing synthetic media — most will not — but because they will be covering stories where synthetic media is a factor, and they need to be able to evaluate it, trace it, and report on it accurately.

The critical process question for synthetic media

Description asks: is this what it claims to be? Analysis asks: how was this produced, and by whom, and with what intent? These are not trick questions — they are the basic moves of verification applied to a new category of evidence. The tools for answering them are developing, imperfect, and worth knowing.

Swap-out · If you teach film, music, or creative industries

AI-generated music, voice cloning, visual effects, and script drafting are live questions in every creative industry right now — about authorship, labor, compensation, and what it means to make something. The synthetic media questions in journalism have direct parallels in every creative field.

The Backlash as a Beat

By spring 2026, the public response to AI — energy costs, data center siting, job displacement, children's safety concerns, the collapse of poll numbers — had itself become a major journalism story. This is worth naming in a journalism course because it illustrates something the critical process is designed to surface: the same technology that is being used to produce and distribute news is also the subject of some of the most significant news of the moment.

A journalism student following the AI beat is not just learning about a technology. They are learning to cover a story that involves: corporate interests and their relationships with government; community-level resistance with genuine local stakes; a scientific and technical domain most general journalists are underprepared to cover accurately; and a political realignment that is scrambling the usual partisan lines. That is a demanding beat — and a significant one.

The backlash story will look different by the time any given student is reading this. The specific controversies will have moved. The poll numbers will have shifted. The technology will have changed. What will not have changed is the need to cover it with the same discipline the critical process applies to any beat: describe what is actually there, analyze how it works and how coverage is shaping public understanding, interpret what it means in light of what you know, evaluate who bears the costs, and engage with what you have found.

The Questions That Hold Across Any Moment

Whatever AI is doing in your field right now — and it will be doing something different in six months — these questions are the right ones to be asking:

  • What decision is this tool making, and who is accountable for that decision?
  • What does this tool optimize for, and what does it not have access to?
  • Where does human judgment remain irreplaceable — and is it actually being applied there?
  • What is the transparency standard for disclosing AI's role to the audience?
  • Who bears the costs of this tool's deployment, and are those costs being covered in the public record?

These are journalism questions. They are also media literacy questions. They are the same questions.

Reading List — Small Version · One module, recurring ritual · Tier 1 spine + Tier 2 field example · Journalism home base

Reading List — Small Version

How to Use This List

The Small version is a recurring module, not a sequential course. The reading list is therefore organized as a menu rather than a week-by-week sequence. Instructors select from Tier 1 for conceptual grounding — these readings are stable and can be assigned in any order. Tier 2 gives one journalism example per critical move; instructors in other disciplines swap this for a field-specific equivalent.

For a one- or two-session stand-alone version of the module, assign one Tier 1 piece before class and use the Tier 2 example as the shared text for the session itself. For the recurring ritual version across a semester, distribute the Tier 1 readings across the first few weeks and return to them as touchstones throughout.

On keeping this list current

Tier 1 readings are relatively stable — foundational texts that hold across time. Tier 2 examples are field-specific and time-sensitive. Instructors should verify Tier 2 links each term and update the field example to reflect current practice. The slot is the constant; the content moves.

Instructor note — on Tier 2

Each example below shows AI operating in journalism for the move it is paired with. These are chosen because they are substantive, accessible, and model the kind of critical attention the move requires. Verify currency each term. If you teach a field other than journalism, the swap-out note tells you what to look for in your own field.

For Description — Seeing What Is Actually There
Associated Press. "AP's Automated Journalism: How We Use Automation to Produce More Stories." ap.org/about/press-releases. Verify link · Update if AP publishes newer guidance
AP's own account of how it uses Wordsmith to automate earnings reports. The transparency here is unusually good — AP discloses what the tool does and what human editors still do. Useful for Description because it models what accounting for a production process actually looks like.
Swap-out by field

Marketing/PR: Find your ad agency or platform's disclosure of how AI tools generate copy variants. Finance: Bloomberg's account of its AI-assisted reporting tools. Science: A journal's disclosure of AI use in literature review or data analysis. The question is always the same: what does the tool do, and what does the human still decide?

For Analysis — How It Works and How It Got Here
Poynter Institute. "What newsrooms need to know about AI tools right now." poynter.org. Update annually — Poynter publishes AI guidance frequently
Practical overview of how AI tools are currently being used in newsrooms and what editorial questions they raise. Good for Analysis because it traces the structural decisions newsrooms are making about AI adoption — not just whether to use it, but how, under what standards, with what disclosure.
Swap-out by field

Find the professional association or trade publication in your field that is actively developing AI standards: AMA for marketing, AICPA for accounting, AIA for architecture, MPAA for film. The structural question — who is making the rules and what do they say — is the Analysis move applied to the field's governance of AI.

For Interpretation — What Your Knowledge Reveals
Bell, Emily and Taylor Owen. "The Platform Press: How Silicon Valley Reengineered Journalism." Tow Center for Digital Journalism, Columbia University, 2017. Available at towcenter.org. Stable — structural analysis, not time-sensitive
The argument that platform distribution has changed what journalism can be, not just how it is delivered. Disciplinary knowledge for journalism students: what the shift from editorial to algorithmic distribution means for what gets covered and who decides. Pairs well with the Interpretation move's question: what does your training let you see that a general reader would miss?
Swap-out by field

Find the key scholarly or professional text in your field that explains what is structurally different about the AI moment — not hype, but a genuine account of what changed and why it matters for practitioners. This is the disciplinary knowledge that makes Interpretation possible.

Course Introduction — Updated Section: "A Note on the Moment"

What to Add — and Where

The following section replaces or supplements the existing "A Note on the Moment" paragraph at the end of the Course Introduction in all three Canvas files. It is written to hold across whatever the cultural conversation about AI looks like when students encounter it — not tied to the specific controversies of spring 2026, but anchored by them.

In the Canvas shell, this section goes between the "What Has Not Changed" block and the "How This Course Is Organized" block.

The Section — Paste Directly

Section heading: A Note on the Moment

This curriculum was developed in spring 2026. At that moment, public attitudes toward AI were shifting measurably and quickly — faster than pollsters had seen with any comparable technology. Protests had blocked data center construction across the country. A sitting U.S. senator called constituents "under siege." An industry executive described opposition as coming from "cave people." A commencement speaker at a major university was booed for praising AI's potential.

By the time you are reading this, the specific controversies will have moved on. The technology will have changed. The poll numbers will look different. AI will be more embedded in daily life in some ways and more contested in others. The moment this paragraph is describing will feel, to you, like recent history rather than current events.

That is fine. It is exactly the situation this curriculum is designed for.

The questions this course asks do not depend on whether AI is popular or unpopular in any given news cycle. They depend on paying attention to something that will not change regardless of the mood: AI is a force in the information environment. It is not the only force. Understanding how information is made, distributed, evaluated, and believed — and practicing that understanding deliberately — is what media literacy has always been about. The AI moment makes it more urgent. It does not make it different in kind.

One more thing worth naming plainly: this course does not take a position on AI. It does not ask you to distrust it, celebrate it, or be afraid of it. It asks you to apply the same critical moves to stories about AI that you apply to any other subject — and to notice, as you do, that AI is simultaneously a tool for making information, a subject of information, and a force shaping how information reaches you. That triple role is unusual and worth understanding carefully. This course gives you the practice to do that.

For Instructors — On Keeping This Current

The specific examples in the first paragraph of this section (protests, data centers, the "cave people" comment, the booed commencement speaker) are from spring 2026. Update them each term with whatever the equivalent live example is. The structure of the paragraph holds — a moment of heightened public attention, a set of positions in conflict, the observation that by the time students read this it will have moved on. The specific examples should always be recent enough that students recognize them.

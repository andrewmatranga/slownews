Unit 1 · Description — Week 1 of 2
Learning to Describe: What Is Actually There
Before you can analyze, evaluate, or act on the news, you have to see it clearly.

Overview and Purpose

Unit: 1 of 5 — Description
Week: 1 of 10
Rhythm: Reading · Journal · Discussion
"Description is the most underrated intellectual act. Before you can argue with the news, explain it, or do something about it — you have to be able to say, precisely and honestly, what it says."

This week introduces the foundation of the entire course: the five-step critical process that structures every unit. We begin with the first and most essential step — description. Description means setting aside reaction, opinion, and interpretation long enough to account for what is actually in front of you. It is harder than it sounds, and it is where most news consumption goes wrong.

The critical process you practice this week is not a checklist to complete once. It is a discipline to develop over ten weeks. Each unit builds on the last, moving from seeing the news clearly (description) to acting on it responsibly (engagement). This week, you will practice description on a piece of news content — without yet worrying about what it means or whether it is true.

You will also begin identifying your news beat — the field, discipline, or topic area that you will follow for the rest of the quarter. Your beat is not an assignment topic. It is your ongoing relationship with a living area of public life.

 

Learning Outcomes

DefineExplain the five-step critical process (Description, Analysis, Interpretation, Evaluation, Engagement) and its logic as a sequence.
Practice DescriptionApply description as a discrete critical act to a news text: account for what is present without interpretation or evaluation.

Before You Begin: A Note on Description

Choose any news story published in the last 48 hours — on any topic, from any source you normally use. Read it once. Then, before doing anything else, write down in plain sentences: What happened? Who is involved? When and where? What evidence is cited? Do not yet ask whether you believe it, agree with it, or think it matters. Just say what it says. Bring this to your journal entry and discussion.

Beat Journal — Week 1

Purpose of the Beat Journal

The Beat Journal is a recurring weekly assignment throughout the course. It is not a summary of readings. It is a record of your developing relationship with your news beat — what you are noticing, what is changing, what you do not yet understand. Think of it as a reporter's notebook crossed with a scholar's reflection. It should be honest, specific, and written in a voice you will recognize as your own when you read it back at Week 10.

Entries are evaluated for specificity, intellectual honesty, and evidence of genuine engagement with the beat — not for polish or certainty. Short and precise is better than long and vague.

 

Week 1 Journal Prompt

Journal Entry — 200–300 words

Describe your chosen beat in plain terms. What field or topic area are you following? Where does it live in the news — which outlets, which reporters, which institutions drive its coverage? Then describe one news item from your beat this week using only description: what happened, who is involved, what evidence is cited. Do not yet evaluate or interpret. End with one question your description leaves unanswered.

Submit to Canvas before the week's discussion session. Entries are read by the instructor and may be selectively shared (anonymized) in discussion.

Unit 1 · Description — Week 2 of 2
Finding and Sourcing Your Beat
Mapping where your field's news actually lives — from primary sources to mainstream headlines.

Overview and Purpose

Unit: 1 of 5 — Description
Week: 2 of 10
Deliverable: Unit 1 Assignment — Beat Source Map

Every discipline has its own information architecture. A story about a pharmaceutical trial travels a very different path than a story about a currency policy or a cultural preservation effort — different primary sources, different specialist publications, different mainstream outlets, and different points at which the original evidence gets simplified, dramatized, or lost entirely. This week, you map that architecture for your own beat.

Description is not only about reading individual news items carefully. It is about understanding the system that produces them: who generates the original evidence or event, who covers it first and with what expertise, and how the story changes as it moves from specialized to general audiences. You cannot evaluate news accurately until you understand where it came from.

The Beat Source Map you produce this week will serve as a reference document for the rest of the quarter. You will return to it as you track a specific story or thread across Units 2 through 5.

Map SourcesIdentify the primary source layer, specialist publication layer, and mainstream media layer for your chosen beat.
Trace a StoryFollow one item from your beat backwards from a mainstream headline to its originating source, noting what changes at each step.
Apply DescriptionDescribe the source ecosystem of your beat — its key institutions, publications, and reporters — without evaluating their quality or bias.
Establish Your ThreadIdentify the specific story, issue, or ongoing development within your beat that you will follow for the remainder of the quarter.

Unit 1 Assignment — Beat Source Map

Purpose

The Beat Source Map is a structured document — not a list, but a description — of the information ecosystem surrounding your chosen beat. It establishes your baseline understanding of how news is produced and distributed in your field, and it will anchor your work for the next eight weeks. Think of it as the orientation document a new journalist writes before covering a beat for the first time.

Instructions

Write a Beat Source Map of 400–500 words organized around the following structure. Use prose, not bullet lists. Write as if you are describing the beat to someone intelligent but unfamiliar with it.

1
The Beat: Name your field or topic area and describe, in one paragraph, what kind of news it generates and why it matters to public life.
2
The Source Layers: Describe the primary source layer (who generates original evidence or events), the specialist layer (who covers it first with expertise), and the mainstream layer (where the general public encounters it). Name specific institutions, publications, or outlets at each layer.
3
The Trace: Describe one story from this week, traced backwards from its mainstream presentation to its primary source. What changes at each step?
4
Your Thread: Name the specific ongoing story, issue, or development within your beat that you will follow for the rest of the quarter. Describe it using description only — what is happening, according to available evidence, as of this week.

Canvas Page — Week 3 Overview
Unit 2 · Analysis — Week 1 of 2
Learning to Analyze: Watching the News Cycle Move
A story on Day 1 is not the same story on Day 14. Analysis tracks the change.

Overview and Purpose

Unit: 2 of 5 — Analysis
Week: 3 of 10
Rhythm: Reading · Journal · Discussion
"Analysis begins where description ends. Once you know what a story says, you can ask how it works — how it changed over time, whose perspectives it includes, and what structural forces are shaping its arc."

Analysis is the second step of the critical process, and it asks a fundamentally different kind of question than description. Description asks: what is there? Analysis asks: how does it work, and how did it get to be this way? In news terms, analysis means watching a story across time — from the breaking moment to the follow-ups, the corrections, the deepening investigations — and asking how the coverage shifts, who drives those shifts, and who is present or absent from the account.

This week you will practice two forms of analysis that recur throughout the course: timeline analysis (tracking how a story develops over days or weeks) and voice analysis (identifying whose perspectives are represented in the coverage and whose are not). Both are skills that journalism students, social scientists, historians, and engaged citizens need — and that require the patience that fast news culture works against.

Define AnalysisExplain the difference between description and analysis in the critical process, and articulate what makes analysis a distinct intellectual act.
Timeline AnalysisTrack how a specific news story changes from its initial breaking moment to subsequent coverage, identifying the forces driving those changes.
Voice AnalysisIdentify the sources and perspectives represented in a piece of news coverage, and name at least two that are absent.
Apply to Your BeatApply both forms of analysis to a current item in your beat thread.

Unit 2 Assignment — Story Arc Analysis

Purpose

The Story Arc Analysis asks you to treat your beat thread as a moving object — not a fixed text but a developing artifact that changes in response to new evidence, competing voices, and editorial decisions. The goal is an analytical account of how the coverage has worked, not an evaluation of whether it has been good journalism.

Instructions

Write a Story Arc Analysis of 450–550 words in prose. Reference at least four specific coverage items from your beat thread (articles, reports, broadcasts, or posts, with dates). Your analysis should make at least one arguable analytical claim about the shape of the coverage — not a description of events, but an argument about how the coverage has worked.

1
The Arc: Map the development of your thread across its coverage. What were the key turning points? What drove them — a new document, a new source, an event?
2
The Voices: Who has spoken in this coverage? Who has been described but not heard? What structural reasons might explain the pattern?
3
The Pattern: Identify one recurring structural feature of how your beat covers this story. Name it as specifically as you can.
4
The Analytical Claim: In two to three sentences, state your analytical argument about this coverage arc. This is not an evaluation of quality — it is an argument about how the coverage works.

Unit 3 · Interpretation — Week 1 of 2
Learning to Interpret: Connecting Headlines to Knowledge
Interpretation asks what it means — and uses your disciplinary knowledge to answer that question.

Overview and Purpose

Unit: 3 of 5 — Interpretation
Week: 5 of 10
Rhythm: Reading · Journal · Discussion
"Interpretation is where the scholar enters. You are not just a reader of the news — you have disciplinary knowledge, theoretical frameworks, and specialized vocabulary that allow you to say something about what a story means that a general audience cannot."

Interpretation is the third step of the critical process, and it is where your academic training becomes directly relevant to public life. To interpret a news story is to use what you know — from your courses, your field's literature, your professional preparation — to explain what it means in a way that goes beyond what the story itself says.

Interpretation is distinct from evaluation. You are not yet judging whether the coverage is good or whether the situation is right or wrong. You are bringing knowledge to bear on meaning. A chemistry student who can connect a news story about industrial pollution to the specific molecular mechanisms their coursework covers is interpreting. An accounting student who can explain the structural logic behind a corporate financial failure using classroom frameworks is interpreting. A student of any discipline who can translate technical jargon into precise, accessible language for a general audience is practicing the most civic form of interpretation there is.

Define InterpretationDistinguish interpretation from both description/analysis and evaluation; explain why disciplinary knowledge is required for genuine interpretation.
Apply KnowledgeUse at least one concept, framework, or body of knowledge from your discipline to interpret a current item in your beat thread.
TranslateRestate a piece of technical or specialized content from your beat in language accessible to a general reader without losing accuracy.
Distinguish Meaning from JudgmentProduce an interpretation of your thread that makes claims about meaning without yet making claims about right and wrong.

Unit 3 Assignment — Interpretive Brief

Purpose

The Interpretive Brief is a document that explains your beat thread to a general, intelligent audience — not by summarizing the news, but by bringing to bear the specialized knowledge that makes the story legible in a way general coverage cannot. Think of it as what a genuine expert would add to a public conversation: not opinion, not evaluation, but the precise application of knowledge to meaning.

Instructions

Write an Interpretive Brief of 450–550 words addressed to a general, intelligent reader — a curious non-specialist. 1

3
The Interpretive Claim: In one paragraph, make your interpretive argument: what does your thread actually mean, in light of what you now know? This is your scholarly contribution.

Unit 4 · Evaluation — Week 1 of 2
Learning to Evaluate: Ethics, Equity, and the Human Stakes
Evaluation asks who wins, who loses, and who pays — and uses evidence, not just feeling, to answer.

Overview and Purpose

Unit: 4 of 5 — Evaluation
Week: 7 of 10
Rhythm: Reading · Journal · Discussion
"Evaluation is where the citizen enters fully. You are no longer only a student of the news — you are a person with values, responsibilities, and a stake in what happens next. Evaluation means saying, carefully and with evidence, what is right and what is wrong."

Evaluation is the fourth step of the critical process and the most morally demanding. It asks you to make judgments — about the news coverage itself, about the events the news describes, and about the implications for real people's lives. But evaluation in the scholarly sense is not the same as having a reaction. It requires the foundation that description, analysis, and interpretation have built: you can only evaluate well what you have seen clearly, tracked across time, and understood in context.

This week introduces two forms of evaluation that run throughout the course: ethical evaluation (what is right or wrong, fair or unfair, about what is happening or how it is covered?) and equity evaluation (who bears the costs and who captures the benefits when events unfold in your beat's field?). Both require looking beyond the story's protagonists — the named institutions, officials, and experts — to the communities and individuals at the margins of the coverage.

Define EvaluationDistinguish evaluation from reaction and from interpretation; explain what makes a scholarly evaluation different from an opinion.
Ethical EvaluationApply an ethical framework to a specific development in your beat thread, making a judgment supported by evidence.
Equity AnalysisIdentify who bears costs and who captures benefits in a specific news development, using concrete evidence rather than generalization.
Evaluate CoverageMake a specific evaluative claim about the adequacy of your beat's coverage — not whether you like it, but whether it serves public understanding.

Unit 4 Assignment — Equity and Ethics Assessment

Purpose

The Equity and Ethics Assessment is a structured evaluative argument about your beat thread. It asks you to move beyond description, analysis, and interpretation to make a reasoned judgment — about what is at stake for real people, whether the distribution of costs and benefits is fair, and whether the coverage adequately serves public understanding of those stakes.

Instructions

Write an Equity and Ethics Assessment of 500–600 words. It should make two central evaluative claims: one about the events or developments in your thread, and one about the adequacy of the coverage. Each claim must be supported by specific evidence and grounded in a named principle or standard. The assessment must also engage with a significant counterargument.

1
The Stakes: Who is affected by the developments in your thread? Name them specifically. What do they stand to gain or lose?
2
The Evaluative Claim: Is the distribution of costs and benefits in your thread fair or unfair, according to a principle you can name and defend? Support your claim with specific evidence.
3
Coverage Evaluation: Does your beat's coverage adequately represent the equity and ethical dimensions you have identified? If not, what is missing and why might it be absent?
4
The Counterargument: State the strongest objection to your evaluation and respond to it substantively.

Unit 5 · Engagement — Week 1 of 2
Learning to Engage: From Scholar to Citizen
Knowledge without action is a private luxury. Engagement is where it becomes a public good.

Overview and Purpose

Unit: 5 of 5 — Engagement
Week: 9 of 10
Rhythm: Reading · Journal · Discussion
"Every field has a public. The question is whether the people who know the most are willing to speak clearly enough for that public to hear them — and to act."

Engagement is the final and most ambitious step of the critical process. Having described, analyzed, interpreted, and evaluated, you now ask: what do I do with what I know? How do I use my disciplinary knowledge and my understanding of this beat to contribute to a smarter, more informed public conversation?

This does not mean simply having an opinion. It means producing something — a piece of writing, a public explanation, a civic argument — that carries your scholarly knowledge into the world in a form that other people can actually use. It means writing with enough clarity and conviction that someone outside the university would find it worth reading. And it means committing to the idea that citizens who understand things deeply have a responsibility to share that understanding.

"The citizen scholar is not someone who knows a great deal. It is someone who uses what they know in the service of a public that needs it. You have spent ten weeks building the right to say something. Now say it."

This is the final week of The Citizen Scholar. You have now followed your beat for eight weeks, applying each step of the critical process to produce a description of your field's news ecosystem, an analysis of how a specific thread has developed, an interpretation of what it means in light of your disciplinary knowledge, and an evaluation of its ethical and equity dimensions.

The capstone is a public-facing piece that synthesizes this work into something a general audience can actually use: an op-ed, an explainer, a letter to the editor, or a short public essay. It should carry the weight of everything you have learned about this thread — but wear that weight lightly enough that a non-specialist finds it worth reading.

Selected capstone pieces will be shared in our final session as examples of what it looks like to bring scholarly attention to the news cycle. Students who wish to submit their piece for publication — in the student newspaper, a community outlet, or a public forum — are encouraged to do so. The instructor will provide feedback for publication-ready revisions.

Canvas Page — Capstone Assignment (Due End of Week 10)

Capstone Assignment — The Public Piece

Purpose

The Public Piece is the culminating work of The Citizen Scholar. It asks you to demonstrate that ten weeks of careful attention to a news beat produces something — a perspective, an argument, a contribution to public understanding — that would not exist without that sustained work. It is not a research paper. It is not a course reflection. It is a piece of public writing: addressed to a real audience, in a real form, about something that matters right now.

Instructions

Write a public-facing piece of 600–800 words. Choose one of the following forms, based on what best fits your argument and your audience:

Form Options

Op-Ed: A first-person argument about your beat thread, addressed to a general readership and making a clear, debatable claim with supporting evidence.

Public Explainer: A precise, accessible account of what your beat thread is actually about — for a reader who has seen the headlines but needs a scholarly guide to understanding them.

Letter to the Editor: A specific, evidence-based response to a piece of coverage in your beat — correcting, deepening, or challenging a claim in the public record.

Regardless of form, your piece must: make an original contribution (not just summarize); carry evidence drawn from your quarter of beat work; be written in a voice appropriate for publication; and be specific enough that a reader knows exactly what you are talking about and why it matters.

 

Submission

Submit the following to Canvas:

  • Your completed Public Piece (600–800 words)
  • Your complete Beat Journal (all ten entries, compiled into a single document)
  • A one-paragraph process note (not assessed for quality, only for completion): describe the form you chose, who your intended audience is, and one thing you would revise if you had more time.

A Note on What You Have Done

You followed a corner of the world for ten weeks. You learned to see it clearly, to track how it moved, to understand what it meant, to judge what was at stake, and to say something about it that was worth saying. That is not a course requirement. It is a practice — one that democratic life needs more people to sustain. The beat does not end at Week 10. The question is whether you keep following it.

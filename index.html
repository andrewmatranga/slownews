A Modular Media Literacy Curriculum

Slow News,
Smart Citizens

Democratic life in the age of generative AI — and five critical moves to navigate it.

Slow News, Smart Citizens is a modular media literacy curriculum built around a five-step critical process. It runs in fifteen minutes or across a full semester. It drops into any course, in any discipline, without restructuring anything. It asks students to slow down, apply the moves, and contribute what only their particular education and attention can add.

Why Now
Generative AI has collapsed the information source chain. The questions media literacy has always asked are more urgent than ever — and harder to practice without deliberate structure.

What This Is and Why It Exists

The Old Problem — and the Old Answer

In 1922, Walter Lippmann described an unavoidable gap between "the world outside and the pictures in our heads." He was writing about newspapers, but the observation has never stopped being true. The representations of events that reach us through media are never the events themselves. They are shaped by who covered them, what got left out, who the audience was assumed to be, and what the economics of the medium required.

Media literacy — the practice of reading those representations critically rather than transparently — emerged as a formal educational project in the 1970s and 1980s, largely in response to television. Educators in the UK, Canada, Australia, and the United States developed frameworks for helping students understand that media texts are constructed, that they carry values and assumptions, and that they represent the world in particular ways for particular purposes.

The core questions those frameworks asked remain exactly right today: Who made this? For whom? What is left out? What does it assume? What would a different perspective reveal?

"The goal of media literacy has never been suspicion. It has been the capacity to ask the next question — and to keep asking even when the system makes that difficult."

The Current Rupture

For most of the internet era, information literacy had a workable architecture. You searched for something, found a page, read it, evaluated the source. The chain from claim to origin was visible.

Generative AI search collapses that architecture. When you ask a question and receive an answer synthesized by a large language model, the source disappears. There is no page to evaluate, no byline to check, no publication whose editorial standards you can weigh. There is a plausible-sounding answer, presented with confidence that does not correlate reliably with accuracy.

This does not make the traditional answers to the authority question obsolete. It makes them more necessary. The practices that have always grounded credible journalism — named sources, verifiable documents, transparent methodology, editorial accountability — are exactly what AI-generated content tends to lack. Those tools are worth understanding, defending, and practicing in a moment when they are under pressure.

Trust and Authority in the AI Era

AI introduces at least three distinct challenges to the existing frameworks of authority: the authorship problem (who is accountable for AI-generated claims?), the provenance problem (can you trace the chain from answer to evidence?), and the volume problem (when the cost of producing plausible content approaches zero, what happens to the friction that quality journalism has always depended on?).

By spring 2026, public trust in AI had declined sharply and rapidly. This matters for journalism because AI tools are now embedded in newsroom workflows, search products, and content distribution. A public that distrusts AI may extend that distrust to journalism that uses it — whether journalists want them to or not.

AI in the Field — The Range

The public conversation about AI in journalism tends to collapse into two positions: AI is destroying journalism, or AI is saving it. Both are wrong in the same way — they treat a set of specific, varied tools as a single phenomenon.

AI is doing several distinct things right now: automating routine content at scale, augmenting investigative capacity, introducing new synthetic media challenges, and generating a public backlash that is itself a major story. This curriculum asks students to apply the same critical moves to stories about AI that they apply to any other subject — and to notice that AI is simultaneously a tool for making information, a subject of information, and a force shaping how information reaches you.

The field examples in the reading list are written from journalism, with every discipline-specific element marked for swap-out. The questions remain the same across any field. The examples are what instructors update each term.

This Curriculum Does Not Take a Position

It does not ask students to distrust AI, celebrate it, or be afraid of it. It asks them to slow down, apply the moves, and contribute what their particular education and attention can add. The AI moment makes that practice more urgent. It does not make it different in kind.

The Five Moves

Applied in sequence, every session. The sequence is the pedagogy — each move requires the one before it.

1
Description
"What does this story actually say?" — Account for what is present: the main claim, the evidence cited, the gap between headline and body. Without reaction. Without evaluation. Just what is there.
Small · Medium · Large
2
Analysis
"How does it work, and how did it get here?" — Trace the structure: who produced it, how it was distributed, whose voices are present and absent. This is where the AI question lives — how did this reach me, and what did AI do to it along the way?
Small · Medium · Large
3
Interpretation
"What does it mean, in light of what you know?" — Bring your disciplinary training, your field-specific knowledge, your educated attention. This is the move where your education becomes a contribution rather than background.
Small · Medium · Large
4
Evaluation
"Is it fair, adequate, and who pays the costs?" — Make a reasoned judgment, grounded in evidence, named by principle, specific about who bears costs and who captures benefits.
Medium · Large
5
Engagement
"What do you do with what you know?" — Produce something. Carry what you have learned into a form a public audience can use. The citizen who has done the first four moves has earned the right to say something.
Medium · Large
A Note on the Moment — Spring 2026

This curriculum was developed in spring 2026. At that moment, public attitudes toward AI were shifting measurably and quickly — faster than pollsters had seen with any comparable technology. Protests blocked data center construction. Poll numbers dropped. A commencement speaker at a major university was booed for praising AI's potential.

By the time you are reading this, those specific controversies will have moved on. That is fine. It is exactly the situation this curriculum is designed for. The questions it asks do not depend on whether AI is popular or unpopular in any given news cycle. They depend on paying attention to something that will not change: AI is a force in the information environment. It is not the only force. Understanding how information is made, distributed, evaluated, and believed — and practicing that understanding deliberately — is what media literacy has always been about.

S · Small

Fifteen Minutes. Three Moves. Any Course.

The smallest version of the practice. Drop it into any existing course, in any discipline, without restructuring anything. The ritual is self-contained — the practice is the point.

Format Recurring ritual or stand-alone session
Time 15 min/week or 1–2 dedicated sessions
Moves Description · Analysis · Interpretation
Assignments Reading Log · Reflection · Self-Assessment

What It Is

Every session opens the same way: one student brings something they read, the class spends fifteen minutes with it using the first three critical moves, then class continues. Over a semester, that practice builds something — a room that arrives prepared, notices more, and reads the news as people who know something rather than passive consumers.

It can also run as one or two dedicated stand-alone sessions: an introduction to the three moves, a shared reading, and the AI conversation opened explicitly. The Reading Reflection makes this version gradable with minimal overhead.

The AI conversation enters naturally through Move 2. Analysis asks where a story came from and how it traveled to the reader. In 2026, that question almost always opens onto algorithmic recommendation, AI summarization, or synthetic content — and students recognize it immediately as describing their own experience.

The Three Moves in Practice

1
Description
"What does this story actually say?"
2
Analysis
"Where did it come from, and how did it reach you?"
3
Interpretation
"What does your knowledge add?"

For Instructors

No syllabus restructuring required. The ritual is self-contained. You participate — not facilitate — by bringing your own answers to the three questions. The AI conversation does not require expertise in AI; it requires curiosity about a question students are already living with. The discipline-specific entry point for Interpretation is where your expertise matters most: what does your field know about this story that the coverage does not say?

Adapts easily for lecture courses (ritual at session start), large enrollment (call-and-response Description, pair Analysis, open Interpretation to the room), online and hybrid sections (async Reading Log + Discussion Board + persistent AI Thread), and any discipline.

Discipline Entry Points · Interpretation

Sciences / Engineering: "What does the methodology of the underlying study actually say — and what did the headline do to it?"

Social Sciences / Humanities: "What historical or structural context does this story assume the reader has?"

Business / Law / Policy: "What does this story leave out about how decisions are actually made in this area?"

Arts / Design / Film: "How is this story framed rhetorically and visually — and what would different choices have produced?"

The AI Thread — Runs All Semester

One persistent Discussion Board thread. Any time a student encounters AI's visible role in how a news item was generated, summarized, recommended, or distributed — they add one observation. One sentence on what they found; one sentence on how they knew (or how they weren't sure).

Over a semester, the thread becomes a class record of how AI is actually operating in the information environment students navigate. More specific, more varied, and more useful than any single reading about AI and media.

M · Medium

Five Weeks. One Story. All Five Moves.

Students commit to one live news story and follow it across the full critical process — producing three pieces of writing that build toward something only five weeks of attention makes possible.

Format Five-week sustained module
Moves All five + Return to Description
Assignments Source Map · Interpretive Note · Short Public Piece · Self-Assessment
Fits Any course with five weeks of available thread

What It Is

The Medium version asks students to commit. Choose one live news story — developing right now, traceable to a primary source, connected to something you know — and follow it for five weeks. Each week applies the next critical move. The assignments build on each other. By Week 5, students have something to say about their story that no one who encountered it only in a headline could say.

The sixth move — turning Description back on their own writing before submission — is what makes the Medium version feel complete rather than just longer. Students apply to their own Short Public Piece the same discipline they have been applying to news. That is both a revision strategy and a lesson in accountability.

The Five-Week Arc

Week 1
Description — Story Source Map
What does this story say? Map the source architecture. Trace the AI footprint. (300–400 words)
Week 2
Analysis — Reading Log
How has it moved? What changed, what drove the change, what did AI do along the way?
Week 3
Interpretation — Interpretive Note
What does your knowledge reveal? The contribution only your training can make. (400–500 words)
Week 4
Evaluation — Reading Log
What is at stake? Who bears costs the coverage is not naming?
Week 5
Engagement + Return to Description
Short Public Piece (400–500 words) + Description applied to your own writing before submission.

The Interpretive Note

The most important piece students write in the Medium version. It asks them to say what their story means in light of what they know — in a way the coverage itself cannot. The standard: could someone without this student's training, their field, their weeks of following this story have written this? If yes, go further. The Note earns its place when it says something only possible because of what the student brings. That is the contribution.

This is where the educated capacity to perceive what is actually there — as distinct from what a summary told you was there — becomes explicit. Students often discover they know more than they thought. The Note is where that discovery gets written down.

What Makes a Good Story
  • Live — developing now, new information arriving
  • Traceable — primary source can be found
  • Connected — to the student's major, field, or experience

Not too broad ("climate change"), not already resolved, not so opinion-laden that Evaluation collapses into reaction before Description has been done. If a story goes quiet in Week 2, that silence is itself data — and belongs in the Interpretive Note.

For Instructors

Return the Interpretive Note with feedback before Week 5. Students need it to write the Short Public Piece. A Brief without feedback produces a Piece without a contribution. That feedback is the most important teaching moment in the Medium version.

The minimum viable Medium version is three weeks: Description (Source Map), Interpretation (Interpretive Note), Engagement (Short Public Piece). Analysis and Evaluation fold into the Reading Log prompts.

L · Large

Ten Weeks. One Beat. The Class Makes Something Together.

The full Citizen Scholar arc. A notebook running underneath everything, three cycles of five moves, cluster formation, and a collective capstone the class assembles in the final session.

Format Ten-week semester layer
Structure Three cycles · Nine moves · Notebook throughout
Assignments Source Map · Interpretive Brief · Equity Assessment · State of the World · Notebook Archive
Capstone The State of the World — assembled Week 10

What It Is

The Large version asks students to follow a beat — an ongoing area of public life connected to their field or their experience — for ten weeks. The five critical moves run in three cycles. Each cycle returns to earlier moves at a deeper level: applied to longer arcs of coverage, to the work of other students, and finally to the collective document the class builds together.

The notebook is the spine. Every week, before class, students write a brief entry in whatever form the instructor names for their discipline: reporter's notebook, researcher's log, field journal, observer's record. The name is the instructor's to choose. The practice is the same — writing toward what you are noticing, not what you are supposed to say.

The Three-Cycle Arc

Wks 1–2
Cycle 1 — Seeing (Description + Analysis)
Choose the beat, map source architecture, trace the AI footprint, identify structural patterns.
Wks 3–4
Cycle 2 — Judging (Interpretation + Evaluation)
Interpretive Brief + Equity Assessment. What your knowledge reveals. Who bears costs.
Wks 5–6
Cycle 2 — Deeper (Clusters Form)
Interpretation and Analysis return at scale. Students find classmates whose beats share their underlying question.
Wks 7–8
Cycle 3 — Contributing (Engagement Begins)
Evaluation returns as civic argument. Clusters draft State of the World entries. Equity Assessment due.
Wks 9–10
Cycle 3 — Assembly
Peer review. Description applied to the collective entry. Assembly session. The document exists.

Cluster Formation

In Weeks 5 and 6, students find the classmates whose beats raise the same underlying question — not the same surface topic, the same deeper inquiry. A student following pharmaceutical pricing and a student following agricultural subsidies may share a question about how public policy serves private interests. That is a cluster. Two to four students. One State of the World entry. A collective argument none of them could have made alone.

The State of the World

The document the class produces together in Week 10. Each cluster contributes one entry addressing the shared question their beats illuminate. The complete document is assembled in the final session. Each cluster reads one passage aloud and names one question their beat work left genuinely open. Then the document is distributed. Then it exists — a record of what this class was watching, at this moment, and what it understood.

The assembly session is the most important class meeting of the course. Protect it.

The Notebook — Weekly Structure

The name is the instructor's to choose. The practice is the same every week:

What I noticed
One specific observation — not a summary. Something that surprised, confused, or changed how you see the thread.
What moved
One thing that changed this week and what you think drove it.
What I'm sitting with
The question you cannot yet answer — specific enough that someone could help you think about it.

Submitted complete at Week 10. Graded holistically — as an arc, not entry by entry.

The Assembly Session

Each cluster reads one passage aloud and names one open question their beat work left genuinely unresolved. The room listens. The document is assembled and distributed. The instructor reads the opening aloud — or invites a student to.

Protect this session. The State of the World is not complete until the class gathers around it. Everything before it is preparation. The assembly is the thing itself.

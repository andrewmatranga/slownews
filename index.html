Slow News,
Smart Citizens

A modular media literacy curriculum for democratic life in the age of generative AI — and a practice for the coffee klatch, the deliberate conversation, the slow read.

Andrew J. Matranga, EdD · Principal Investigator
Media, Film & Journalism Studies
Center for Ethical Artificial Intelligence · 2026

This is a curriculum for learning to read carefully in a world that makes careful reading hard. It runs in fifteen minutes or across a full quarter. It fits inside any course, in any discipline, from ninth grade through graduate school. It asks students to slow down, apply five critical moves to the news, keep a hand-written notebook, and assemble a physical publication at the end of the module.

The links below are the complete project. Start with the README if you are an instructor. Start with the site if you want the overview first. If you are a student whose instructor assigned this, start where they told you to — and if they did not say, start with the site.

Today

The Five Moves, Applied Right Now

On the day this curriculum is published, Pope Leo XIV released Magnifica Humanitas — "On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence" — his first encyclical and the Catholic Church's formal entry into the AI governance conversation. He signed it on May 15, the 135th anniversary of Rerum Novarum, Leo XIII's foundational 1891 encyclical on labor and capital during the first Industrial Revolution. He presented it in person — the first pope to personally present an encyclical — alongside an Anthropic co-founder.

This is not religious instruction. It is a media literacy exercise in progress:

Description: What does the document actually argue? The title is "Magnificent Humanity" — the framing centers human dignity, not AI danger.
Analysis: Why today? Why this co-presenter? Who is the primary source and what are the specialist and mainstream layers doing with it?
Interpretation: What does the timing — 135 years after the first Industrial Revolution encyclical — tell a student of media history or religious studies?
Evaluation: Who bears the costs this document names, and who is absent from the framing?
Engagement: What would you add to the public record about this story that the coverage does not say?

The AI conversation has left Silicon Valley and journalism schools. It is now being addressed by the oldest continuous institutional tradition in the Western world, on the same day a university journalism professor is publishing a media literacy curriculum. That is the state of the world on May 25, 2026.

The Five Critical Moves
1
Description
What does this actually say?
2
Analysis
How does it work, and how did it get here?
3
Interpretation
What does it mean, in light of what you know?
4
Evaluation
Is it fair, and who pays the costs?
5
Engagement
What do you do with what you know?

This curriculum is built for reuse and adaptation. If you teach the Medium or Small version in a field other than journalism and develop strong field-specific examples, share them. The goal is adoption, not proprietary control.

What You Need to Start

A notebook. Physical. Unlined or lightly lined. At least 40 pages for the Large version. A three-dollar composition book is fine.

A beat. An ongoing area of public life connected to your field or your life. Live, traceable to a primary source, generating news each week.

Lippmann. Public Opinion, Chapter 1 — "The World Outside and the Pictures in Our Heads." 1922. Public domain. Free online. Five to seven pages. Assign it before the first session.

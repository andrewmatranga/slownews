Instructor Page — Course Overview (pin to course top)
Slow News, Smart Citizens · Large · Instructor Overview
Ten Weeks. One Beat. Nine Moves. One Document the Class Makes Together.
The full Citizen Scholar arc — three cycles of three, a notebook running underneath everything, and a capstone that belongs to the class.

What This Is

Slow News, Smart Citizens (Large) is the full Citizen Scholar framework: ten weeks, five Campbell critical moves applied across three cycles, a weekly notebook that runs from Week 1 through Week 10, four individual assignments, and a collective capstone — the State of the World — assembled by the class in the final session.

The Small version gave students three moves applied once to a single item. The Medium version extended those three moves across five weeks on one live story and added Evaluation and Engagement. The Large version runs all five moves across ten weeks on a self-chosen beat, returns to earlier moves at deeper levels, adds cluster formation and collaborative production, and ends with the class making something together that none of them could have made alone.

"The beat does not end at Week 10. The question the course leaves students with is whether they keep following it — and what it means to be the kind of person who does."

Three Cycles — Nine Moves

The Large version runs three cycles of three moves each. Each cycle returns to moves the student has practiced before and applies them at a deeper level — to a longer arc of coverage, to the work of other students, and finally to the collective document the class is building.

The Notebook

The notebook is the backbone of the Large version. Every week, before class, students write a brief entry — in whatever form the instructor names for their discipline. Reporter's notebook. Researcher's log. Field journal. Observer's record. The name is the instructor's to choose; the practice is the same: writing toward what you are noticing, not what you are supposed to say.

The notebook runs from Week 1 through Week 10. It is submitted complete at the end of the course and assessed holistically — as a record of sustained attention, developing perception, and honest engagement with a corner of the world across ten weeks. It is not graded entry by entry. It is read as an arc.

Instructor Page — How to Run the Large Version

How to Run the Large Version

The Weekly Rhythm

Every class session opens with fifteen minutes of conversation — the same ritual as Small and Medium, now running on beats rather than single items. The instructor's opening question changes each week to track the current cycle's move. Students arrive with their notebook entry completed. The conversation moves through the room: what is your beat doing this week? What moved? What did you notice that others in the room are not watching?

The instructor's role across ten weeks is to participate, to listen for connections students do not see, and to name one connection per session that the room missed — but only after giving the room time to find it first. By Week 5, the room is finding connections the instructor did not plan. By Week 8, cluster conversations are happening before class starts. By Week 10, the instructor's job is to hold the space for the assembly and get out of the way.

The Arc — Instructor Tasks by Week

Wk 4

Clusters meet for the first time. Their task: one sentence naming the shared question across their beats. The instructor circulates and pushes: "Is that the real shared question, or the surface one?"

Each cluster reads one passage aloud and names one open question. The room listens as citizens encountering reporting they have not seen. The document is assembled and distributed. The instructor reads the opening aloud — or invites a student to. Then it exists.

Forming Clusters

Clusters form naturally through the notebook entries and class conversation of Weeks 3–5. The instructor's task is to see the connections before students do and propose groupings that reflect genuine intellectual rhymes across beats — not surface topic similarities. A student following environmental contamination and a student following corporate labor practices may share a deeper question about accountability that neither has named yet. That is a cluster.

Cluster size

Two to four students. Groups of two produce tighter, more focused entries. Groups of four produce richer, more contested ones. Both work. What does not work: a cluster formed for convenience rather than shared inquiry, or one where one student's beat clearly dominates the others.

The State of the World format

Decide the format before Week 8 so the Cluster Proposal can name it. Options: a shared Google Doc (one section per cluster); a printed and assembled zine (one page per cluster, assembled in the final session); a Canvas wiki with linked entries; a formatted PDF distributed at assembly. The format matters less than the fact that it is real — a document that exists, with a name, that the class made.

Instructor Page — The Notebook (permanent reference; share with students)

The Notebook

What It Is and What to Call It

The notebook is the weekly writing practice that runs underneath every other assignment in the Large version. Its name is yours to choose for your discipline and your students. A journalism course might call it a reporter's notebook. A research methods course might call it a researcher's log. A science course might call it a field journal. A literature or cultural studies course might call it an observer's record. A media studies course might call it a critical log.

The name shapes how students understand the practice. Choose the name that makes the act of writing toward what you notice feel natural in your discipline — not like an assignment imported from journalism education, but like the record-keeping your field has always valued.

The one thing the name cannot change

The notebook is not a summary of readings. It is not a report on what happened in the news this week. It is a record of what the student noticed — what surprised them, what confused them, what changed between last week and this week, what question they are sitting with that they cannot yet answer. It is writing toward perception, not writing toward compliance.

The Weekly Prompt Structure

Each week's notebook prompt follows the same three-part structure, calibrated to that week's move. Students write 150 to 250 words — enough to say something specific, not enough to summarize everything. The prompt is not a checklist. It is a starting point. Students who find themselves writing past the word count because something is genuinely interesting should keep writing.

The three-part weekly structure

What I noticed: One specific observation from this week's beat work — not a summary, a noticing. Something that surprised you, confused you, or changed how you see the thread. What moved: One thing that changed in the story or the coverage this week — and what you think drove the change. What I'm sitting with: The question you cannot yet answer. Specific enough that someone could help you think about it — not "I wonder about AI" but "I can't figure out why the primary source disappeared from coverage after Day 3."

Assessment

The notebook is submitted complete at the end of Week 10 — all ten entries, in chronological order, compiled into a single document. It is assessed holistically, not entry by entry. The standard is sustained genuine engagement: did the student show up to their beat each week and write toward what they actually noticed? A notebook that is honest and specific across ten weeks will score higher than one that is polished and generic.

What to look for holistically

Does the thinking develop across ten weeks? Does the student's language for their beat become more precise over time? Do the questions they are sitting with in Week 9 reflect what they learned in Weeks 1 through 8? Is there evidence that the notebook entries informed the formal assignments — that the map, the brief, the assessment, and the State of the World entry grew from the notebook rather than alongside it?

Holistic grade

Complete and genuinely engaged across all ten weeks: full credit. Mostly complete with some thin or summary-style entries: 80%. Significant gaps or entries that are clearly perfunctory: 60%. Incomplete: no credit. Do not grade individual entries — reading the arc holistically takes less time and produces better feedback.

Instructor Page — The AI Thread Across Ten Weeks

The AI Thread

How AI Runs Across the Three Cycles

In the Small version, AI entered once inside Analysis and was held lightly. In the Medium version, it deepened across five weeks. In the Large version, it is a thread that runs through all three cycles and all five moves — students accumulate ten weeks of evidence about how AI shapes what they see, and the State of the World entry implicitly addresses that accumulation whether or not the cluster makes it explicit.

Cycle 1 — Weeks 1–2: AI in the source architecture

Students map where AI enters their beat's information system — in how stories are found, summarized, recommended, distributed. The Beat Source Map includes an AI audit. This is structural observation, not alarm.

Cycle 2 — Weeks 3–6: AI and meaning

The Interpretive Brief asks what AI summarization flattened or missed. The Equity Assessment asks whether AI-mediated coverage adequately represents who bears costs. Cluster formation sometimes surfaces shared AI patterns across beats — students following different fields noticing the same algorithmic behavior in different contexts.

Cycle 3 — Weeks 7–10: AI and the document

The State of the World entry is, among other things, a human-authored, sourced, disciplinarily grounded document produced in response to an AI-shaped information environment. Students do not need to say this explicitly. The assembly session is the moment where that implicit argument becomes visible — a room of people who followed things carefully, together, and made something with it.

The Framing — Use It Once, Return to It When Earned

For Week 1 — set this up and let it run

This course is organized around a paradox. The tools reshaping your information environment — AI-generated content, algorithmic recommendation, large language models producing plausible text on any subject in seconds — are also among the most significant drivers of economic growth in a generation. Your institution's endowment may hold positions in companies whose products are making it harder to know what is true.

This is not a reason for cynicism. It is a reason for the kind of careful, slow, disciplined attention this course asks of you. The notebook you keep across ten weeks is a response to that paradox. So is the State of the World the class produces together. A room of people who looked carefully at different corners of the world and then looked at what they found together — that is what democratic information culture needs, and it is increasingly rare.

By Week 10, you will have ten weeks of evidence about how AI shaped what you saw. The assembly session is where you use it.

Instructor Page — Adapting the Large Version

Adapting the Large Version

The Non-Negotiables

Three things cannot be compressed out of the Large version without losing what makes it distinct from Medium. The notebook must run all ten weeks — it is the connective tissue that makes the State of the World meaningful. The Interpretive Brief must be graded and returned before Week 5 — students need that feedback to deepen their interpretation in the cluster work. And the assembly session in Week 10 must happen — the State of the World is not complete until the class gathers around it.

Everything else is adjustable.

If You Are Running Large After Medium

Students who completed the Medium version already have a live story, a Source Map, an Interpretive Note, and experience with all five moves. The Large version picks up directly: their Medium story becomes their Large beat thread. Weeks 1 and 2 of Large compress to one week of onboarding — students present their existing Source Map and thread to the class, and beat selection is complete. Cluster formation can begin earlier, sometimes as early as Week 4.

For Courses That Cannot Run All Four Individual Assignments

The minimum viable Large version keeps the Interpretive Brief and the State of the World entry, and folds the Beat Source Map and Equity Assessment into the notebook prompts rather than separate graded assignments. This reduces the grading load while preserving the intellectual arc. The notebook becomes more substantive in Weeks 1–2 (Source Map embedded) and Week 4 (Equity thinking embedded), and the Interpretive Brief remains the individual assignment that anchors the course.

For Online and Hybrid Sections

The cluster work is the most challenging element to adapt for async courses. Clusters should be formed by Week 5 and given a dedicated async collaboration space — a shared Google Doc, a Slack channel, a Canvas group. The Cluster Proposal due at Week 8 serves as the synchronizing mechanism: it forces clusters to reach consensus on their shared question and entry plan before drafting begins. The assembly session in Week 10 can run as a synchronous video session even in an otherwise async course — it is worth the scheduling effort. If synchronous is impossible, a shared document thread where each cluster posts their passage and open question, and other clusters respond, is the closest equivalent.

What This Course Asks

The Small version asked you to bring something you read. The Medium version asked you to follow one story for five weeks. The Large version asks you to follow a beat — a field, a live area of public life connected to something you know — for ten weeks, applying nine moves across three cycles, and then to sit with a few classmates whose beats rhyme with yours and make something together that none of you could have made alone.

That last part is the State of the World. It is the document this class produces in Week 10. It is what sustained attention, applied by different people to different corners of the same world, eventually makes possible.

"The citizen scholar is not someone who knows a great deal. It is someone who uses what they know in the service of a public that needs it."

The Notebook

The notebook runs from Week 1 through Week 10. Your instructor has named it for your discipline — reporter's notebook, researcher's log, field journal, observer's record, or something else. Whatever it is called, it is the same object: a weekly record of what you noticed, what moved in your beat, and what question you are sitting with that you cannot yet answer.

The notebook is not submitted weekly. It is submitted complete at Week 10 — all ten entries, as an arc. It is graded holistically: not entry by entry, but as evidence that you were genuinely present to your beat across ten weeks and that your thinking developed over time. Read your Week 1 entry before you write your Week 10 entry. Something will have changed. The notebook is where that change lives.

The State of the World

In Weeks 5 and 6, you will find the classmates whose beats raise the same underlying questions as yours — even if your subjects look nothing alike. That is your cluster. Together you will produce one entry for the State of the World: a piece of writing that addresses the shared question your beats illuminate, that draws on all of your individual work, and that could not have been written by any one of you alone.

In Week 10, the class assembles all the cluster entries into the State of the World. Each cluster reads one passage aloud and names one question their beat work left open. Then the document exists — a record of what this class was watching, at this moment, and what it understood.

Student Page — Finding Your Beat

Finding Your Beat

Beat vs. Story

A story is a specific event or development. A beat is the field that produces stories — the ongoing area of public life that journalists return to week after week because it keeps generating news. The Medium version asked you to follow a story. The Large version asks you to follow a beat.

The practical difference: a story can go quiet. A beat does not. Even when your specific thread stalls or resolves, the beat keeps producing material. By Week 10, you will have followed a corner of the world long enough to see its patterns — what it reliably covers, what it consistently ignores, how AI shapes what surfaces and what disappears.

Beat examples — by discipline

Sciences: Environmental contamination in your region. Clinical trial coverage in your specialty. Energy technology reporting. Social sciences: Housing policy in a specific city. Criminal justice reform in your state. Immigration enforcement at a specific institution. Business/economics: Labor organizing in a specific sector. Cryptocurrency regulation. A specific company's financial and labor practices. Humanities/arts: Coverage of a cultural institution, preservation effort, or artistic community. Language policy in a specific country. A literary or cultural controversy with ongoing implications. Any field: How your field's research is covered by mainstream media — and how AI is changing that coverage.

The Thread Within the Beat

Once you have a beat, find a thread — the specific ongoing story within the beat that you will follow most closely across the ten weeks. The thread is your anchor. It is what you bring to the notebook each week, what you trace in the Beat Source Map, and what you will know better than almost anyone by Week 10.

The thread should be live and specific: something developing right now, with a traceable primary source, connected to something you know. It can shift slightly across the quarter as the story develops — that is following the beat, not losing the thread.

The Notebook Starts Now

Your first notebook entry is due before Week 1's class. It does not need to be polished. It needs to be honest. Name your beat, name your thread, describe what it says right now in two or three sentences, and name the question you are starting with. That question may change by Week 3. That is the point.

The Standard for Interpretation

There is a kind of knowledge that comes from looking carefully at something long enough to see what is actually there — as distinct from what you expect to see, or what a summary told you was there. Your field gives you a lens that changes what you see when you apply it to a story. The Interpretive Brief asks you to name what that lens reveals.

The test: could someone without your training, your field, your weeks of following this beat have written this? If yes, go further. The Brief earns its place when it says something that is only possible because of what you bring.

Assessment — Beat Source Map (due end of Week 2; 400–500 words)

Assessment — Beat Source Map

Purpose

The Beat Source Map establishes the information architecture of your chosen beat — the three source layers, the structural pattern of coverage, the AI footprint, and your disciplinary angle. It is the anchor document for everything that follows. A careful Source Map in Week 2 makes the Interpretive Brief, the Equity Assessment, and the State of the World entry significantly easier to write with depth.

Instructions — 400 to 500 words in prose

1
Name the beat and the thread. One sentence each: what is the beat (the ongoing area of public life), and what is the specific thread you are following within it right now?
2
Map the three source layers. Primary source (who or what generates the original material), specialist layer (who covers it first with expertise), and mainstream layer (where the general public encounters it). Name specific institutions, publications, or outlets at each layer.
3
Trace the AI footprint. Where does AI enter your beat's information system — in recommendation, summarization, generation, distribution? What specific observation can you make about what AI does to your beat's coverage?
4
Name your disciplinary angle. One sentence on what your field or background connects to this beat — specific enough to seed the Interpretive Brief in Week 3.
5
Name one structural pattern you already see. Something about how this beat's coverage works that you noticed in the first two weeks — not a judgment about quality, an observation about structure.
ElementWhat We Are Looking ForPoints
Beat and threadBoth named specifically and in present tense. Thread is live and traceable.15 pts
Three source layersAll three layers described with named examples. Primary source located or attempted.30 pts
AI footprintAt least one specific, concrete observation about AI's role in this beat's information system.25 pts
Disciplinary angleOne sentence specific enough to seed interpretation — not "I study economics" but "my training in [specific area] lets me see [specific thing] in this beat."20 pts
Structural patternOne structural observation — specific, descriptive, not evaluative.10 pts
Total100 pts
Assessment — Story Arc Analysis (folded into Week 4 Equity Assessment in Large; standalone if instructor chooses)

Assessment — Story Arc Analysis

Purpose and Note

In the Large version, the Story Arc Analysis from the Medium version is folded into the Equity Assessment — the evaluative arc across Weeks 3 and 4 already covers the analytical ground the Story Arc addressed in the Medium version. Instructors who want to assign the Story Arc Analysis as a standalone piece (for example, in courses running Large after completing Medium without producing the Medium's Analysis assignment) can use it independently after Week 4.

The standalone Story Arc Analysis asks students to make an analytical argument about how their beat's coverage has worked across the first four weeks — not what the story is about, but how the coverage functions. At least four coverage items must be cited with dates. The central deliverable is one arguable analytical claim about the shape of coverage.

Instructions — 400 to 500 words

1
The arc. Map the development of your thread across its coverage so far. What were the key turning points? What drove them?
2
The voices. Who has spoken in this coverage? Who has been described but not heard? What structural reasons might explain the pattern?
3
The pattern. One recurring structural feature of how your beat covers this thread — named specifically.
4
The analytical claim. Two to three sentences stating your argument about how the coverage works — not an evaluation of quality, an argument about structure.

Assessment — Interpretive Brief (due end of Week 3; 500–650 words)

Assessment — Interpretive Brief

Purpose

The Interpretive Brief is the most important individual assignment in the Large version — the one the instructor must return with feedback before Week 5, because students need it to deepen their interpretation in the cluster work and to seed the State of the World entry. It extends the Medium version's Interpretive Note: longer, more developed, and now applied to a beat rather than a single story.

The Standard — Elevated for the Large Version

The Interpretive Brief in the Large version carries more weight than the Medium version's Note because it is doing more work: seeding the cluster's shared question, informing the Equity Assessment, and ultimately shaping the State of the World entry. The standard is the same — could someone without your training have written this? — but the stakes of the answer are higher. A Brief that is genuinely specific about what disciplinary knowledge reveals about a beat's meaning will generate better cluster connections, richer evaluative arguments, and a more substantive State of the World entry than one that is vague about its interpretive contribution.

Instructions — 500 to 650 words

1
What the coverage says the beat is about. Accurately describe what the mainstream coverage presents as the beat's meaning. Use Description.
2
What your knowledge reveals. Name and explain the concept, framework, or field-specific understanding that changes how this beat reads. Apply it specifically: what does the coverage miss or misrepresent that your training allows you to see?
3
Your interpretive claim. What does this beat actually mean, in light of what you know? Specific, arguable, only possible because of what you bring.
4
The underlying question. What question does your interpretation open that it does not close? This is the seed of your cluster's shared question.
ElementExpectationsPoints
Coverage accountAccurate, specific description of what mainstream coverage presents as the beat's meaning15 pts
Disciplinary knowledgeNamed, explained accessibly, applied specifically — a lens that changes what you see, not background35 pts
Interpretive claimSpecific, arguable, only possible because of this student's training and beat work35 pts
Underlying questionSpecific enough to seed a cluster's shared inquiry — not a general wonder, a real open question15 pts
Total100 pts

Assessment — Equity Assessment + Cluster Proposal

Purpose

Two deliverables this week — one individual, one collective. The Equity Assessment closes the individual arc of the course with a structured evaluative argument. The Cluster Proposal opens the collective arc: the group's plan for the State of the World entry.

Individual — Equity Assessment (500–600 words)

1
The stakes. Who is affected by the developments in your beat? Name them specifically. What do they stand to gain or lose?
2
The evaluative claim. Is the distribution of costs and benefits in your beat fair or unfair, according to a principle you can name and defend? Support with specific evidence.
3
Coverage evaluation. Does your beat's coverage adequately represent the equity and ethical dimensions you have identified? What is missing, and why might it be absent?
4
The counterargument. State the strongest objection to your evaluation and respond to it substantively.

Assessment — State of the World Entry (due end of Week 10; 500–700 words per cluster)

Assessment — State of the World

Purpose

The State of the World entry is the culminating collective assignment of the Large version. It is a piece of writing that could only exist because these specific students followed these specific beats at this specific moment and then looked at what they found together. The entry is not a collection of individual summaries. It is a collective argument — one that requires all of the cluster's beats to make its case, and that could not have been made by any one student alone.

The Standard for the Entry

Ask: could a reader who has never heard of any of these beats encounter this entry and find it worth reading? Does it make a claim — not just describe a situation? Does it draw on the evidence of all the beats in the cluster, not just the most prominent one? Does it say something that ten weeks of collective attention made possible, and that a single week of reading the news would not?

If the answer to any of these is no, revise before submitting.

Instructions — 500 to 700 words; submitted as a cluster

Revise the Week 9 draft in response to peer feedback. The final entry should be self-contained — a reader who has never encountered any of your individual work should be able to read it and understand what your cluster was watching, why it mattered, and what you collectively understood about it that most people do not.

The Assembly Session

Each cluster takes three to four minutes in the final session. One passage read aloud — not the strongest sentence, the one that captures what your cluster was watching and why it mattered. Then one open question: the genuine question your beat work raised that ten weeks of attention did not resolve. The room listens. The instructor (or a student) reads the opening of the complete document aloud. Then it is distributed. Then it exists.

A closing note for instructors

The assembly session is the most important class meeting of the course. It is also the most likely to be cut when time gets tight. Protect it. The State of the World is not complete until the class gathers around it — until the document exists in the room, read aloud, with the people who made it. Everything before it is preparation. The assembly is the thing itself.

Assessment — Notebook Archive + Self-Assessment (due end of Week 10; holistic)

Assessment — Notebook Archive + Self-Assessment

The Notebook Archive

Submit all ten notebook entries in chronological order, compiled into a single document. Each entry labeled by week. The notebook is assessed holistically — not entry by entry, but as evidence that you were genuinely present to your beat each week and that your thinking developed over time.

Before you compile, read back through all ten entries. Something will have changed — in your language for the beat, in the specificity of your questions, in what you find surprising versus expected. The Self-Assessment is where you name what changed.

Holistic grading standard

Complete and genuinely engaged across all ten weeks, with evidence of developing thought: full credit. Mostly complete with some thin or summary-style entries: 80%. Significant gaps or entries clearly written for compliance rather than noticing: 60%. Incomplete: no credit.

The Self-Assessment — Nine Questions, One Per Move

Answer these nine questions — one for each of the nine moves across the three cycles. A sentence or two each. More if you have more to say. Completion-graded: submit honestly and you receive full credit.

Cycle 1 · Description

What did you believe about your beat at Week 1 that turned out to be wrong, incomplete, or more complicated than you understood?

Cycle 1 · Analysis

What structural pattern in your beat's coverage did you see by Week 2 that you could not have named before following it?

Cycle 2 · Interpretation

What is the most specific thing your training lets you see in your beat that a general reader would miss? Name it precisely.

Cycle 2 · Evaluation

Who bore a cost in your beat that the coverage did not name adequately across ten weeks? What would have needed to change?

Cycle 2 · Interpretation + Analysis Deeper

What did your cluster's shared question reveal about your own beat that you could not have seen alone?

Cycle 3 · Evaluation + Engagement

What does the State of the World entry argue that none of your individual assignments argued? What required the cluster to make it possible?

Cycle 3 · Engagement Collective

What did you have to revise or give up from your individual framing to write something genuinely collective?

Cycle 3 · Description Returns

When you applied Description to the State of the World entry — what did it actually say versus what you thought it said? What changed in revision?

Cycle 3 · Engagement Assembly

What question are you leaving the course with — something ten weeks of careful attention raised but did not resolve? And are you going to keep following it?

For instructors — on receiving self-assessments

The ninth question — are you going to keep following it — is not rhetorical. Students who say yes and mean it are the ones the course most succeeded with. Students who say no honestly have still learned something worth knowing. Read both kinds. The proportion of each, across a class, tells you more about how the course ran than any other single data point.
